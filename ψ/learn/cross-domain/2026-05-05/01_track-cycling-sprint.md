# Track Cycling Sprint S&C Cross-Applied to 50m Freestyle Swimming

**Author**: Aree
**Date**: 2026-05-05
**Sources**: 18 (UCI, USA Cycling, peer-reviewed F-V profiling literature, coach interviews, athlete-attributed numbers)
**Premise tested**: Of all land sports, track sprint cycling — not track running — is the closest neuromuscular and temporal analog to a 50m freestyle. It is the only land event that combines (a) ATP-PCr dominance, (b) 10-13s peak-power task duration, (c) **zero impact**, and (d) closed-kinetic-chain triple extension into a fixed external resistance. Swim S&C almost never taps it.

> **Brain-routing note**: This doc complements `ψ/learn/strength-training/2026-05-05/01_track-sprint-cross-app.md` (track *running* lineages — Francis, Pfaff, Schexnayder, McMillan, Yessis). It does NOT repeat them. Track cycling has its own coaching tree (Dyer, van Eijden, Grace, Kordi, Haak), its own science (Dorel, Gardner, Mornieux), its own benchmarks (25 W/kg peak, 2.3-2.5× BW squat), and its own peaking model (Olympic-quad block periodization). All of those are the subject here.

---

## 1. Executive summary — what this doc adds

- **Track cycling sprint is the only mainstream land event that is impact-free AND ATP-PCr-pure.** This makes it categorically different from track running for masters athletes and rehab returnees: the same CNS/RFD stimulus without the eccentric-impact cost.
- **Elite track sprinters squat 2.1-2.5× BW and gym 3-4×/wk year-round** — including in-season — which contradicts the "drop strength as competition approaches" template most swim programs use (UCI, 2024; USA Cycling, 2024).
- **The 5-second standing start in track cycling is mechanically the closest land analog to a swim block start that exists** — both are pure horizontal force production from rest into a fixed resistance, both depend on triple extension, both are decided in <500ms of contact time.
- **Peak power testing in cycling is decades ahead of swimming.** UCI WCC protocol (2× 6s peak, 30s sprint, 4-min aerobic) on a Wattbike or SRM gives a force-velocity-fatigue profile in ~20 minutes. The swim equivalents (semi-tethered F-V, 25m max kick, 10s catch-pull on a Vasa) exist but are barely used.
- **Force-velocity profiling research in cycling has 25 years of data behind it** (Dorel et al., 2005; Mornieux et al., 2022). Pmax, F0, V0, and optimal cadence are diagnostic tools that swim coaches could borrow wholesale — the math is identical.
- **High-cadence ergo work (130-150 rpm) is the closest dryland mimic of high-stroke-rate swim sprinting** that exists. McEvoy already does similar work; British/Dutch sprint cyclists have 30 years of programming wisdom on it.
- **Track cycling does NOT have an upper-body / shoulder analog to swim** — this is the honest limit. The trunk and arms in track cycling are isometric stabilizers; in swim they're the prime movers. Lower-body protocols transfer; upper-body programming must come from elsewhere (gymnastics, throwing sports, swim-specific work).

## 2. Why track cycling sprint is the closest land analog

The 50m freestyle (~21-24s elite) is metabolically and neuromuscularly closest to two land events:

| Event | Duration | Energy system | Impact | Closed-chain into resistance |
|-------|----------|---------------|--------|-------------------------------|
| 100m sprint (track running) | 9.5-10.5s | ATP-PCr 80% / glycolytic 20% | Very high | No (open chain swing) |
| 200m flying lap (track cycling) | 9.0-10.5s | ATP-PCr ~85-90% | Zero | Yes (pedal/crank) |
| Kilo (1km TT cycling) | 60-65s | ATP-PCr 30% / glycolytic 60% / aerobic 10% | Zero | Yes |
| **50m freestyle** | **21-24s** | **ATP-PCr ~50% / glycolytic ~45% / aerobic ~5%** | **Zero** | **Yes (water resistance)** |

By energy system, the 50m fly + a partial kilo is the right blend; by mechanics, it's the team-sprint man-1 (standing start to ~13s @ peak power, ~6 starts in a session). The two events that share **zero-impact + fixed-resistance + triple-extension** are 50m freestyle and track sprint cycling — they are mechanical cousins.

This matters for masters athletes returning from layoff. Track running cross-application (Francis et al., see sister doc) prescribes flat sprints, hill sprints, and bounding — all eccentric-loaded ground-impact tools. For an age-31 athlete with 9 months of detraining, those carry a 4-6 week tendon-ramp risk. Track cycling protocols carry **none** of that risk — the bike absorbs all the eccentric load, leaving pure concentric/isometric stress. For a swim sprinter with shoulder history or any joint vulnerability, this is a genuinely under-recognized advantage.

## 3. Key training methodologies — the three lineages

### 3.1 British Cycling (1994-2024) — Manchester velodrome system

**Coaching lineage**: Iain Dyer (lead sprint coach 2000-2016, then endurance 2016-2021); Jan van Eijden (sprint coach 2007-2021, double sprint world champion as athlete); Justin Grace (sprint coach 2014-2024, ex-NZ and France); Jason Queally / Shane Sutton in earlier eras. Core training base at Manchester Velodrome since 1994 (British Cycling, 2014; Olympics.ie, 2022).

**Athletes produced**: Chris Hoy (6 Olympic golds), Jason Kenny (7 Olympic golds, the most-decorated British Olympian), Victoria Pendleton (2 Olympic golds), Becky James, Laura Trott (endurance side).

**Methodology characteristics** (synthesized from coach interviews + athlete-attributed training logs):
- **Periodized 4-year Olympic block.** Big strength gains in years 1-2 of the cycle (post-Games), specificity dialed up years 3-4. Within each year, a 4-6 week pre-comp peaking block is standard.
- **Year-round gym, 3-4×/wk** (UCI, 2024). Even in-season. This is sharply different from road cycling and from most swim S&C.
- **Video + power analysis on every sprint session.** Van Eijden was the bridge into modern analytics — bike-mounted SRM/Wattbike sensors paired with race-footage analysis (Wikipedia, 2024).
- **"Gym-first, then track" phase logic**: gym-based neuromuscular force build → track sessions monitored via cadence/speed/split sensors. The track work *cashes in* the gym strength as power.
- **The 95% rule** (shared with track running CFTS lineage but enforced here too): every quality rep is full-effort with full recovery. No sub-maximal "build-up" sprints.

**Justin Grace philosophy**, on record (Cyclingnews, 2014): "There are no secrets — we just do the basics very well, and we do them every day, year after year." This is the same principle Charlie Francis articulated for sprinters; British Cycling executed it on the track for 25 years.

### 3.2 Dutch system (2018-present) — current GOAT factory

**Coaching lineage**: Hugo Haak (national track sprint coach), Mehdi Kordi (head coach track sprint, KNWU). Newer system but already dominant.

**Athletes produced**: Harrie Lavreysen (5× Olympic gold by Paris 2024 — sprint, team sprint, keirin), Jeffrey Hoogland (7× team-sprint world champion, multiple kilo world records), Roy van den Berg (Tokyo 2020 team-sprint gold, world records).

**Methodology characteristics** (from athlete interviews and federation publications):
- **Heavy gym 3-4×/wk, year-round.** Lavreysen back-squats up to 210 kg @ ~91 kg BW = ~2.3× BW (Christian Bosse, 2024). Deadlift, lunge, plyometric variations layered in.
- **Low-cadence force intervals** (~60 rpm against heavy gear) to develop crank torque; this is mechanically equivalent to in-water resistance work for swimmers (high force at low velocity).
- **High-cadence sets** at 130+ rpm — 30-second efforts with short recoveries. Develops the velocity end of the F-V curve.
- **Peak power 2,100W+** for elite men (Lavreysen documented; Olympics.com, 2024). Hoy at 2,500W remains the historical benchmark; Lavreysen's 5-fold Olympic record is achieved at slightly lower peak with better mechanical efficiency (Wikipedia / Grokipedia, 2024).
- **BMX-cycling crossover talent stream.** Lavreysen came from BMX, where the gate start is the most explosive 5-second event in cycling. The Dutch and the British both deliberately recruit BMX-trained kids into track sprint — they arrive with elite gate-start mechanics already wired.

### 3.3 German lineage — Förstemann / van Eijden's home system

**Athletes**: Robert Förstemann (74cm thigh, 280 kg back squat at ~96 kg BW = ~2.9× BW), René Enders, Maximilian Levy, Kristina Vogel (3× Olympic medallist, 11× world champion before her 2018 spinal injury).

**Methodology** (Förstemann interview, UCI 2024):
- **Off-season**: 60-90 reps per workout @ 50-70% 1RM (high-volume hypertrophy/GPP)
- **Pre-competition**: 1-3 reps × 4-6 sets at maximum load
- **Sustained sprint power**: Förstemann famously held >700 W for >1 min — a glycolytic capacity well above pure-sprint specialists (UCI, 2024)

The German approach is somewhat more bodybuilding-influenced than British/Dutch, but the in-season periodization shape is similar: high reps far out, low reps high load close to comp.

## 4. Gym programming specifics

Synthesized from USA Cycling (2024), UCI (2024), Hoy (BikeRadar 2014, PureGym 2024), and Förstemann (UCI 2024):

### 4.1 Frequency, structure
- **3-4 gym sessions/wk year-round.** This is non-negotiable in elite track sprint — a hard contrast to road cycling and most swim programs.
- **2 strength + 1-2 power sessions** per week is typical. Strength = compound heavy lifts; power = Olympic derivatives, jumps, ballistic loaded work.

### 4.2 Heavy compounds — exact prescription

Per the USA Cycling Sprint program reference (2024):

| Lift | Frequency | Sets × reps | % 1RM |
|------|-----------|-------------|-------|
| Back squat | 2×/wk | 4-5 × 1-4 | 80-85% |
| Front squat | (alternated with back squat) | 4-5 × 1-4 | 80-85% |
| Deadlift / trap bar | 1-2×/wk | 4-5 × 1-3 | 85-90% |
| Lunges (loaded) | 1-2×/wk | 3-4 × 6-8/leg | 60-75% |

Off-season opens at 50-70% 1RM × 8-12 reps for 4-6 weeks of base; competition phase pulls into 1-3 rep max work.

### 4.3 Olympic lift derivatives

- **Snatch / clean variations 1-2×/wk, 3-4 × 1-3 reps** (USA Cycling, 2024)
- **Top US Team sprinters can clean 1.2-1.5× BW** as a benchmark
- **Heavy cleans off-season → hang power cleans pre-comp** (lower volume, maintains explosiveness during taper)
- **Velocity-based training (VBT)** is now standard for load-prescription — find the load where peak concentric power output is maximized for that athlete on that lift today

### 4.4 Strength-to-weight benchmarks (elite track sprint)

| Athlete | BW | Back squat 1RM | Ratio |
|---------|-----|----------------|-------|
| Chris Hoy (BikeRadar, 2014) | 92 kg | 240 kg | 2.6× |
| Robert Förstemann (UCI, 2024) | ~96 kg | 280 kg | 2.9× |
| Harrie Lavreysen (Bosse, 2024) | ~91 kg | 210 kg | 2.3× |

The "elite track sprinter squats 2.5× BW" claim is well-supported. Note that Lavreysen — currently the GOAT — runs slightly *lower* on the squat ratio than Hoy or Förstemann; he wins on mechanical efficiency, not raw strength. This is the same lesson Craig McLean (UCI WCC) articulated: "high watts do not necessarily mean you're going to be fast" (UCI, 2024).

For swimmers, the parallel benchmark would be 1.75-2.0× BW trap bar deadlift or 1.5-1.75× BW back squat — track-sprint cyclists are stronger than the strongest documented swim sprinters because their sport rewards it directly. Swim sprinters likely don't need 2.5× BW; the diminishing returns come earlier because water-resistance peak forces are lower than crank torque peaks.

### 4.5 Plyometrics — what cycling sprint adds beyond track running

The track-running doc covers bounding, depth jumps, hill sprints. Track cycling adds a few specifics:
- **Ballistic loaded jumps with chains/bands** at 20-30% 1RM — accentuates the velocity end without spinal compression
- **Single-leg box jumps and step-ups** — the standing-start power phase is alternating-leg, not bilateral
- **Banded over-speed pedaling** (assisted from a partner cable) — high-cadence neural training that has no swim equivalent yet

## 5. Peak power testing protocols

### 5.1 UCI WCC standard protocol (Aigle, Switzerland)

Used to talent-ID sprint vs endurance riders at the UCI World Cycling Centre (UCI, 2024):
1. **2 × 6-second peak power tests** — rolling start, max effort, 5-min recovery between
2. **30-second all-out sprint** (Wattbike) — 60s pre-load at 4.5 W/kg, then go
3. **4-minute aerobic test** — VO2max proxy

Outputs: Pmax (W and W/kg), Pmean-30s, Pmean-4min, fatigue index.

### 5.2 Wattbike 6-second peak power test (industry standard)

Rolling start protocol:
- Pre-load: 60s at 100W or ~50% target (warm-up)
- Sprint: 6s all-out, max gear, max cadence
- 2-3 reps with 3-5 min between
- **Score = best 1-second rolling average within the 6s window**

Elite male track sprinter: 1700-2500W absolute, 22-26 W/kg; UCI elite-male threshold is 25 W/kg (UCI, 2024). Female elite: ~20 W/kg; Anna Meares benchmarked 1800+W (UCI, 2024).

### 5.3 Force-velocity profile from sprint ergometer

Dorel et al. (2005, 2022) established the core methodology, refined by Mornieux et al. (2022; PMC9505270):
- **Method**: 3-4 maximal sprints against varying resistances (or single sprint with broad cadence sweep)
- **Outputs**: F0 (force at zero velocity, N), V0 (velocity at zero force, rpm), Pmax (W), optimal cadence (= 0.5 × max cadence)
- **Elite track sprint values**: F0 ~1380 N, V0 (PRmax) ~265 rpm, Pmax ~1620 W, optimal cadence ~130-132 rpm
- **Linearity is essentially perfect** (R² = 1.0) across gear ratios — the F-V relationship is a robust individual signature, not an artifact of testing setup

### 5.4 Swim-applicable analogs

What from this transfers to a 50m sprinter's testing battery?

| Cycling test | Swim analog | Notes |
|--------------|-------------|-------|
| 6s peak power (Wattbike) | **10s tethered swim peak force** | Already validated (PMC12011652, 2024); equivalent diagnostic |
| F-V profile from sprint ergo | **Semi-tethered swim load-velocity profile** (Olstad/Gonjo) | Methodology adapted; use 1/5/9 kg loads, single-rep max each |
| 30s Wattbike sprint | **15m underwater max + 35m surface, scored as average power proxy** | No direct equivalent; this is the closest |
| Optimal cadence | **Stroke rate at which 25m time minimizes** | Equivalent concept; optimal SR ~50-60 cy/min for elite male sprinters |
| Watts/kg | **Tethered force/kg** | ~0.4-0.5 N/kg for elite sprint swim (limited published data) |

The cycling testing battery is more mature than the swim equivalent because of 25 years of velodrome instrumentation and the structural fact that the bike *is* the ergometer. Swim is catching up — semi-tethered F-V (Olstad, Gonjo, 2020-2024) is the closest in-water equivalent to the Wattbike sprint test.

## 6. Periodization for 10-20 second events

### 6.1 The Olympic 4-year cycle (British Cycling pattern)

- **Year 1 (post-Games)**: GPP base, address athlete-specific weaknesses identified in last cycle's debrief. Heavy gym, lower track volume, technique work.
- **Year 2**: SPP — convert the gym base to power on the bike. Velodrome volume up, gym maintained.
- **Year 3 (Worlds year)**: Build to a major peak at Worlds. Refines the integration. First test of the cycle's specific approach.
- **Year 4 (Games year)**: Another peak at Worlds (often), then a deeper peak at the Games. Includes the planned 6-week pre-Games peaking block.

### 6.2 The 6-week peaking block (used by all elite programs)

- **Weeks 1-2**: Volume drop ~30%, intensity holds, gym shifts to 1-3 rep max work
- **Weeks 3-4**: Velocity emphasis on the bike — high-cadence over-speed work, race-pace sprints with full recovery
- **Weeks 5-6 (taper)**: Volume drop ~50-70%, intensity 100%. Last heavy gym ~10-14 days out. Maintain neural snap with light ballistic work and high-cadence opens.

### 6.3 Block periodization for sprint cyclists (Coyle / Bahnert / Coppel research)

Coppel et al. (2023, ResearchGate 372766389) tracked elite track sprint cyclists across 3× 4-week blocks:
- **Block 1**: Some athletes peak power first; others peak capacity first. **Individual response heterogeneity is the rule, not the exception.**
- **Block 2**: Bridge work — convert whichever you didn't open with
- **Block 3**: Peak — sport-specific power application

Key finding: there is no universal block order that works for every sprinter. Profile the athlete's deficit (capacity-deficient vs power-deficient) and order blocks around closing it. This is the same logic as F-V profiling — diagnose first, prescribe second.

### 6.4 Application to a 6-month swim sprint cycle (Toey-style)

The cycling pattern compresses cleanly into a 6-month build for a single major swim meet:

| Phase | Weeks | Pool | Gym | Cycling-equivalent block |
|-------|-------|------|-----|--------------------------|
| Reintroduction | 1-4 | Technique, easy aerobic | 1×20 base, mobility | Year-1 GPP |
| Strength base | 5-12 | Build aerobic, intro race-pace | 3-4×/wk, 80-85% 1RM, 4-5 × 1-4 | SPP gym phase |
| Power conversion | 13-20 | Race-pace 25/50s, max-velocity work | Maintain strength, add Olympic derivatives, contrast pairs | "Convert the gym" phase |
| Peak block | 21-26 | 90%+ work, full recovery, race rehearsals | 1-3 reps × 3-4 sets, drop volume 30-50% | 6-week peak |
| Taper | last 1-2 | Volume 50-70% down, intensity holds | Light ballistic only, last heavy 10-14d out | Last 14 days |

## 7. Application to 50m freestyle sprint — concrete transferable protocols

### 7.1 Gate start → block start

Both events: pure horizontal force production from rest into a fixed resistance, decided in <500ms of contact.

**Transferable elements**:
- **Reaction-cue training**: BMX/track cycling does this on a $15,000 reaction system at the Manchester velodrome. Swim equivalent: any reaction app on a phone, 3-5 reps × 3-5 sets pre-pool 2×/wk.
- **Triple extension drills**: hang power clean, trap bar jump (already in track-running doc), but specifically the standing-start pattern of "knee-extension drive into immediate follow-up cycle" is unique to gate starts. For swimmers, this maps to depth-jump-to-broad-jump complexes.
- **First-three-strokes power audit**: cycling uses crank-by-crank torque measurement for the first 3 cranks of a sprint (Dorel et al.). Swim equivalent: stroke-by-stroke video with stroke-length and stroke-rate tracking for first 5 strokes after breakout. The principle is identical — measure the acceleration window in fine detail.

### 7.2 F-V testing transfer

Apply the cycling F-V framework directly to the in-pool swim test:
- Run a semi-tethered F-V test (1/5/9 kg loads, single-rep max each)
- Calculate F0, V0, Pmax, optimal load
- Identify which end (force, velocity, or balanced) the athlete leaks on
- Prescribe gym + pool work that closes that end specifically

This is exactly what Mornieux et al. (2022) describe for cyclists; the math is the same. The swim brain already covers F-V profiling in `2026-05-05/02_force-velocity-profiling.md`; this doc adds the cycling-side maturity that validates the method.

### 7.3 High-cadence ergo work as dryland sprint mimic

A cycling sprinter does 30-60s of 130-150 rpm work as a neural drill ("supersprins"). For a swim sprinter, the closest dryland tool is:
- **Wattbike or Watt cycle ergometer at 130+ rpm** for 5-8 × 30s, full recovery
- **Mechanically clean, zero shoulder cost, mimics high-stroke-rate neural patterning**
- **Use 1-2×/wk in build phase**; drop in taper

This is a hidden gem for swim sprinters with shoulder loading limits. McEvoy is documented to use stationary bike sprints in his training (per brain `cameron-mcevoy.md`); the cycling sprint coaching tradition is where the volume and cadence prescriptions have actually been worked out.

### 7.4 Year-round gym

Track sprint cyclists do not de-load gym in-season. Most swim programs do — and the evidence from cycling suggests this is wrong for sprinters specifically. A sprint swimmer should hold 2 × strength sessions/wk all the way through competition phase, only pulling back to 1× and lighter loads in the final 2 weeks. The cycling literature is explicit on this; the swim literature is confused on it.

### 7.5 Peak power tracking as the primary KPI

Cycling tracks Wattbike 6s peak power weekly. The trend on this number is the single best predictor of race readiness. Swim equivalent: track 10s tethered force test weekly (or, if no force plate, max sprint kick-board 25m time at full effort once weekly). Plot the trend. When it stalls or drops, you have a CNS or local fatigue signal — same logic as the Wattbike test.

## 8. What does NOT transfer

Honest section.

- **Trunk and arms in cycling are isometric stabilizers**; in freestyle they are prime movers. Track cycling has no analog for the catch-pull-finish in the water. Upper-body programming for swim must come from elsewhere — gymnastics, throwing, cable-based pull work, swim-specific (Vasa, Halo bench, in-pool). The cycling lineage is silent on this.
- **Closed-chain cycling vs open-chain swim "feel for water"**: a cyclist has zero technical question about where their feet meet the resistance. A swimmer's catch is a moving feel-target that doesn't have a cycling parallel. Don't overlook this — most swim coaches who try to use bike work as a primary modality miss that this is the irreplaceable part.
- **Cadence numbers don't translate directly**. A track sprinter's 130 rpm is 2.2 cycles/sec for two legs; a swimmer's 60 cycles/min is 1 cycle/sec for two arms. Same neural-velocity domain conceptually but the absolute numbers can't be benchmarked across sports.
- **Body composition**: track sprinters typically run 90-100 kg @ ~10-12% body fat with 70+ cm thigh circumference. Swim sprinters run 80-95 kg @ ~8-10% body fat with longer-leverage builds. Trying to add 10 kg of leg muscle to a swimmer to match Förstemann would *cost* swim performance via drag — there's a body-composition optimum that's lower for swim than for cycling. Use the strength benchmarks (W/kg, % BW lifts), not the absolute numbers.
- **Standing-start kilo specificity**: the kilo's first 12s out of the gate is unique even within cycling. It's an acceleration profile no other event matches — peak watts in the first 4-5s, then a defended decay. Swim's 50m has a different shape (peak velocity ~10-20m, then stroke-rate maintenance). Don't try to over-map the kilo opening directly onto a 50m swim — use it as a triple-extension power reference, not a metabolic template.
- **In-season heavy lifting frequency in masters athletes**: 3-4 gym sessions/wk year-round is the elite track-cycling norm but is documented in 23-32-year-old male professionals with full recovery infrastructure. For a 31-year-old masters returnee with 9 months detraining, 2-3 sessions/wk in build phase, 1-2 in peak phase is more realistic. The cycling number is a ceiling, not a floor.
- **High-cadence over-speed cycling does not directly train swim catch mechanics.** It trains neural-velocity capacity. The catch (hand entry, EVF setup, pull pattern) is taught in water with feel-based work. Don't expect the bike to teach swim feel.
