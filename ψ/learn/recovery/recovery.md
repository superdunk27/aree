## Cross-cutting principles (Aree's decision rules)

### From doc 01 (sleep)
- **Sleep duration matters, but timing of cuts matters more** — protect early-night SWS (alcohol/stress hits this) AND late-night REM (early waking hits this); know which you're sacrificing
- **Two-process model** (Borbély) — Process S (sleep pressure builds) + Process C (circadian alignment) → both must align for quality sleep
- **Athletic sleep extension is the most-validated single intervention** in sport science (Mah et al., Stanford basketball: +9% shooting after 5-7 weeks of 10h in bed)
- **Caffeine has a 5-6 hr half-life** — afternoon coffee meaningfully degrades sleep quality even when subjective sleep feels fine
- **Alcohol fragments sleep, especially REM** — 2 drinks within 2h of bed measurably worsens recovery markers next day
- **Light exposure timing > total amount** — bright morning light (10k+ lux outdoors) phase-anchors circadian; dim evening light (<100 lux) protects melatonin onset
- **CBT-I beats sleeping pills** for chronic insomnia (effect sizes ~0.7-1.0; pills habituate and disrupt architecture)
- **Walker's *Why We Sleep* is mostly correct, partly overstated** — Guzey 2019 critique flagged specific mortality claims; core science holds

### From doc 02 (HRV)
- **The "trend not single-day" rule is the #1 most-broken HRV principle in popular use** — single readings are noisy; 7-day rolling average is the actionable signal
- **rMSSD is gold-standard for daily monitoring** — but ONLY with clean signal (chest strap or validated PPG)
- **The Plews paradox** — short-term overreaching can elevate parasympathetic markers (compensatory). HRV interpretation must include training context, not raw value
- **HRV-guided training** beats fixed plans by ~0.5-1.5% in well-controlled studies — small but consistent
- **Wearable accuracy varies hugely** — HRV4Training (peer-reviewed validation), Polar H10 (gold-standard chest strap), Whoop (opaque), Oura (mixed independent results), Apple Watch (improved but variable)
- **Confounders dominate signal** — alcohol (huge), illness, menstrual cycle, breathing rate, body position, stress can each move HRV more than training does
- **Subjective wellness questionnaires are competitive with HRV** — sometimes superior, especially for athletes who can't capture clean morning data
- **ACWR (acute:chronic workload ratio)** — Gabbett's original "1.0-1.3 sweet spot" claim has been challenged; treat as one signal among many, not a hard rule

## How sleep + HRV interlock with prior rounds

Recovery is the bottleneck under all prior work:

- **Round 2 (strength training)** — heavy lifting requires CNS recovery (Charlie Francis High-Low). Sleep is where CNS recovery happens
- **Round 3 (cross-domain breath-hold)** — apnea adaptations consolidate during sleep; poor sleep blunts the response
- **Round 1 (50m swim)** — taper protocol depends on recovery monitoring; HRV/RHR trends signal readiness
- **Round 4 part 1 (skill acquisition)** — memory consolidation is sleep-dependent (SWS for procedural automatization, REM for emotional + creative integration)

Aree's mental model: **train + recover are co-equal halves**. Training quality without recovery quality wastes the stimulus.
