**Premise tested**: 100m sprinters and 50m freestyle swimmers are the closest neuromuscular analogs in all of sport; what does swim S&C miss by ignoring track sprint coaching lineage?

## 1. Executive summary — what this doc adds

- **Charlie Francis's High-Low CNS model is the single most important framework swim coaches don't use.** It maps almost perfectly onto a 50m sprinter's week and would prevent most overtraining seen in age-group-derived swim programs.
- **The "95% rule"** (Francis): everything is either above 95% of max or below 75%; the 76–94% "grey zone" is the most common training error in swimming and produces neither speed nor recovery (Francis, 2008; Dechant, 2010).
- **Bounding** is a horizontal SSC tool that swim plyo programs almost never include but that produces 0.12–0.18s ground-contact times — directly relevant to the eccentric-concentric reversal of the start and turn (Pryor; SimpliFaster, 2024; PMC 7386155).
- **Heavy resisted sled work** (>20% BW; even up to 80–100% BW for elite Jamaican sprinters) develops horizontal force production analogous to the underwater push-off from blocks/walls (Cross et al., 2017; Morin, 2016).
- **Olympic lift derivatives — clean pull, hang power clean — outperform full snatch/clean** for sprint athletes because peak velocity occurs in the 2nd pull, which is shared (Hori et al., 2008). For swimmers with shoulder histories, derivatives sidestep the catch position.
- **Trap bar jumps at 10–20% 1RM** correlate with sprint times more strongly than barbell jump squats and are safer for swimmers' shoulders than overhead Olympic lifts (Turner et al., 2015).
- **Schexnayder's General Strength circuits** offer a low-CNS conditioning modality that could replace the "junk yardage" swim coaches use on recovery days.
- **Yessis's 1x20 program** is a credible long off-season GPP block that builds tendon/ligament resilience without the CNS cost of heavy lifting — a hidden gem for masters swimmers (age 30+) returning from layoff.

## 2. The premise — why track sprint S&C is the most under-tapped resource for swim sprinters

Energy-system contributions for elite 50m freestyle (~21–24s) and 100m sprint (~10s) are the two most ATP-PCr-dominant timed events in non-jumping sport. Both are constrained by:
- Maximal motor-unit recruitment (Type IIx fibers)
- Rate of force development (RFD), not absolute force
- Stretch-shortening cycle (SSC) efficiency in the start
- CNS readiness (no "training through fatigue" — fatigued reps build slow patterns)

Yet swim S&C has historically been built on **rowing/endurance lineages** (long aerobic base + bodybuilding lifts) and more recently on **field-sport S&C** (a generic "strength + plyo + Olympic lift" template). Track sprint S&C — the discipline that has spent 70+ years optimizing 10-second efforts — is the closest analog and is barely cited in swim literature. Cameron McEvoy's recent results (WR 20.88, see brain file `cameron-mcevoy.md`) imply that 50m swim is moving toward a "track sprinter who happens to be in water" model, which is exactly what this cross-application predicts.

## 3. Lineages worth knowing

### Charlie Francis (1948–2010) — CFTS / High-Low
Canadian sprint coach; coached Ben Johnson, Angela Issajenko, Mark McKoy. His book *Speed Trap* and the *CFTS Manual* codified a system built on two principles: (1) the CNS recovers slowly from true high-intensity work, so high days must be far apart; (2) anything in the 76–94% "grey zone" interferes with both adaptation and recovery (Dechant, 2010; SimpliFaster, 2018).

### Dan Pfaff (b. 1953) — ALTIS / sprint mechanics
49 Olympians, 9 medals (Donovan Bailey 100m gold 1996; Greg Rutherford LJ gold 2012). Currently lead coach at ALTIS in Phoenix. Philosophy: each athlete has an individual mechanical solution; the coach's job is to remove movement obstacles, not impose a template (ALTIS, 2024). Famous for "alternate methods" — he uses heavy bag work, Verdun walks, gymnastic rudiments alongside conventional weights.

### Boo Schexnayder — LSU / SAC
44+ years coaching; 26 NCAA champions, 8 Olympic/Worlds medallists. Codified **General Strength (GS) circuits** — calisthenics + hurdle mobility + medicine-ball + weight-room circuits, organized as "preparation for specific work" (Just Fly Performance Podcast, 2024). His multi-throw and multi-jump systems are tendon/elasticity-focused.

### Stuart McMillan — ALTIS CEO
70+ Olympians coached. Strong opinion that **contextual** speed training (e.g., resisted sprints with intent) outperforms isolated speed training (e.g., a heavy squat hoping it transfers). Weight room is a means, not an end (Pacey Performance Podcast #286).

### Dr. Michael Yessis — 1x20
Soviet-influenced sport scientist; the **1x20 program** prescribes 18–25 exercises × 1 set × 20 reps, performed 3x/week, advancing 1.25–2.5 kg/week. Counter-intuitive but produces equal or better sprint/jump improvements vs. traditional programming in athletes without a strength base (Yessis, 2014; Bracius via SimpliFaster).

## 4. Specific protocols transferred to swim sprint

### 4.1 Bounding (alternating + single-leg)
**What it is**: Exaggerated horizontal jumping for distance, alternating legs. Measured by total distance covered in N contacts and ground-contact time (GCT).
**Why it transfers**: Swim block start = horizontal force production with knee-extension drive identical to bound take-off. Surface phase break-out and underwater dolphin kick reversal involve the same SSC pattern. Sprinters achieve GCT 0.12–0.18s during bounding (PMC 7386155); below 0.20s is the SSC threshold associated with sprint-specific power.
**Prescription for swimmer**:
- Beginner: 3 × 5 alternating bounds over 10–15m, technique-focused, GCT not measured
- Intermediate: 4 × 8 bounds over 20–30m; aim for "long, low, fast off the ground"
- Advanced: 4–6 × 10 bounds over 30m; target GCT < 0.18s if you have a contact mat
- **Volume cap**: 30–60 contacts/session beginner, 80–120 contacts/session advanced (Marathon Handbook, 2025)
- **Frequency**: 1–2x/week off-season, 1x/week in-season; 48h removed from heavy lower
- **Coach use**: Replace one of the two off-season plyo days with bounding sets. Pair with a low-volume pool day (kick technique only). Track distance covered in 10 bounds as a proxy for horizontal RFD.

### 4.2 Heavy resisted sled work
**What it is**: Towing or pushing a sled at >20% body mass; loads classified as light (<10%, <10% velocity loss), moderate (10–15%), heavy (15–30%), very heavy (>30%) (Cross et al., 2017; jbmorin.net 2016).
**Why it transfers**: Heavy sled produces horizontal ground reaction force and horizontal impulse exceeding unresisted sprinting. The *push-off from a swim block* and the *push-off from each turn wall* are both pure horizontal force events lasting 0.3–0.5s — far closer to a heavy sled push than to a vertical jump.
**Prescription for swimmer**:
- Heavy push: 6 × 10–15m at 30–40% BW, full recovery (3–4 min)
- Very heavy (advanced): 4–6 × 10m at 70–100% BW (Jamaican-style "march"); only after 12+ weeks of base
- **Frequency**: 1x/week, deep off-season; remove 6 weeks before competition
- **Coach use**: Sled day replaces a deck-based plyo session. Load matches the same horizontal force regime the swimmer will produce off the wall. Cue: chest forward, slow shin angle, drive heel-to-hip — same as block extension.

### 4.3 Hill sprints
**What it is**: All-out runs uphill, 8–12s duration, gradient 6–12% (steeper = more force, gentler = more speed) (TrainingPeaks, 2024; PMC 12214983).
**Why it transfers**: Same physiology as sled — high horizontal force, short eccentric component, low injury risk vs. flat sprints (especially for masters athletes returning from detraining; see Toey's profile). Allows maximal CNS firing without the impact-spike of flat-track work.
**Prescription for swimmer**:
- 6 × 8–10s on a 6–8% gradient, walk-down recovery (~90s) for novices
- 8 × 10–12s on 8–10% for intermediate; 4 min recovery
- **Frequency**: 1x/week; particularly valuable in the 8–4 week pre-comp window when the athlete needs CNS work but high impact is risky
- **Coach use**: For an aging masters sprinter (Toey, 31, 9-month detrained — see brain), hill sprints are arguably the safest entry-point to CNS training in the first 4 weeks of return. Less injurious than flat 100m sprints, more transfer than stationary bike intervals.

### 4.4 Olympic lift derivatives (clean pull, hang power clean)
**What it is**: The pulling portion of a clean/snatch without the catch. Hang power clean = mid-thigh start, triple extension, catch in a high front-rack quarter-squat (Athletic Lab, 2018).
**Why it transfers**: Triple extension (ankle plantarflexion + knee extension + hip extension) is identical to the block start sequence. **Peak bar velocity is in the 2nd pull, which is shared with the full clean — so a swimmer gets the velocity training stimulus without the catch (which is the dangerous part for a shoulder)** (PMC 4993140; Hori et al., 2008).
**Prescription for swimmer**:
- Clean pull: 4 × 3 @ 80–100% of clean 1RM, 1–2x/week off-season
- Hang power clean: 4 × 3 @ 70–80% of HPC 1RM, 1x/week
- **Skip**: Full snatch (overhead catch is high impingement risk for swimmers with prior shoulder pathology; ROI is too low to justify)
- **Coach use**: If a swim S&C coach wants triple extension power without the technical cost or shoulder risk of full O-lifts, hang power clean is the answer. For a masters swimmer with any shoulder history, **clean pull only** (no catch).

### 4.5 Trap bar jumps
**What it is**: Hex/trap bar deadlift performed as a jump, leaving the ground at the top. Loaded at 10–20% of trap-bar deadlift 1RM (Turner et al., 2015; Gymaware/SimpliFaster).
**Why it transfers**: Higher peak power than barbell jump squat; load is at the sides (not on the spine), so axial loading is dramatically lower. Strong inverse correlation with 10m and 20m sprint times in elite rugby players (PubMed 25436621). For swimmers, this is the **single best loaded-jump option** — it preserves shoulder neutrality, doesn't compress the spine like a back-squat jump, and develops vertical/horizontal mixed power.
**Prescription**:
- 4 × 3 reps @ 10–20% trap-bar 1RM, 2 min rest between sets
- **Frequency**: 1–2x/week in off-season; pair with a heavy non-jump lift earlier in the session, OR use as the second component of a contrast pair (see 4.7)
- **Coach use**: Replace traditional barbell jump squats (which load the spine and challenge a hyperkyphotic swim posture). Use as a power-day staple. McEvoy already uses trap bar deadlifts (see brain `cameron-mcevoy.md`); the jump variant is a logical extension.

### 4.6 Schexnayder GS circuits
**What it is**: 12-station circuit, 20s work / 40s rest per station, 12 total sets. Stations are calisthenics + hurdle mobility + medicine ball throws + bodyweight stuff. One Schexnayder variant inserts an all-out 10m sprint at the end of each station (just-fly-sports, 2024).
**Why it transfers**: Replaces "junk yardage" recovery swims with a low-CNS, high-volume, full-body conditioning stimulus that also addresses tissue tolerance and mobility. Especially relevant on Francis "low days" (see §5).
**Prescription**:
- 1–2x/week, low-intensity day, 25–35 minutes
- Stations example for swimmer: push-up variations, hollow body holds, hurdle walk-overs, MB rotational throws, plank reach, glute bridges, A-skips in place
- **Coach use**: This is the *single best replacement* for the "easy 2k recovery swim" that masters coaches still prescribe. Better tissue stimulus, better mobility maintenance, no shoulder volume cost.

### 4.7 Contrast / complex training (French Contrast Method)
**What it is**: Pairing a heavy strength exercise (~85% 1RM, 3 reps) with a plyometric (~6–8 reps) targeting the same muscle group, 3–12 min rest between (NSCA Coach 9.4.3; Wikipedia 2024). French Contrast Method extends this to 4 elements: heavy strength → plyometric → loaded plyometric → assisted/overspeed plyometric.
**Why it transfers**: Post-activation potentiation acutely raises power output for ~5–10 minutes after the heavy lift. McEvoy's coaches already use a French Contrast variant in his gym sessions (per brain `cameron-mcevoy.md` primary source extraction).
**Prescription for swimmer**:
- Pair 1: Trap-bar deadlift 3 × 3 @ 85% → trap-bar jump 3 × 3 (4 min rest between)
- Pair 2: Hang power clean 3 × 3 @ 80% → broad jump 3 × 3
- Pair 3: Heavy sled push 3 × 10m → unresisted 10m sprint
- **Coach use**: One contrast block per power session, off-season only. Removes the need for separate strength and plyo days during compressed weeks.

## 5. CFTS principles applied to swim

### The high-low CNS model for a swimmer's week
Francis's rule: high-intensity work on days that are minimum 48h apart, with low-intensity (true recovery, <75%) or rest in between. **The grey zone (76–94%) is forbidden.** For swimmers this maps to:

| Day | Domain | Detail |
|---|---|---|
| Mon | HIGH | Pool: race-pace 25/50s @ 95–100% with full recovery + dryland: heavy lift |
| Tue | LOW | Schexnayder GS circuit + technique-only pool (≤2k, no rep above 75%) OR full rest |
| Wed | HIGH | Pool: power session (sled-style resistance, broken 50s) + plyo/bounding |
| Thu | LOW | Mobility + light core + easy aerobic skill work, or rest |
| Fri | HIGH | Pool: 100m race-prep set + dryland: contrast pair |
| Sat | LOW | Active recovery — walking, sauna, easy spin |
| Sun | OFF | Full rest |

This is identical in shape to Charlie Francis's microcycle for Ben Johnson — just with pool work substituted for track work on high days.

### The 95% rule
Any pool repeat slower than 95% of best for that distance is either (a) genuine warm-up/recovery (<75%, fine) or (b) the grey zone (76–94%, harmful). The standard "build-up" 50s at 80% effort that swim coaches prescribe are exactly the grey zone that Francis warned against. **Swim coach action**: rewrite intervals so every quality rep is timed and ≥95% of season-best for that distance, with recovery sets that are explicitly easy (<75%). No middle ground.

### Managing CNS — practical signals
- HRV trending down 3+ days → drop the next high day to low
- Reaction time (use a smartphone app) lengthening → CNS fatigued
- Sprint kick board 25m time slowing 5%+ session-to-session → CNS or local muscle fatigue; investigate
- Subjective: heaviness in legs in warm-up = high day not advised (Francis called this "no juice")

## 6. What does NOT transfer

Honest section. Not every track tool earns its place in a swim program.

- **Running gait drills (A-skips, B-skips, dribble drills, wicket runs)**: low ROI for a swimmer beyond general coordination. The ankle/knee mechanics are not present in the freestyle kick. Schexnayder-style hurdle mobility work, however, *does* transfer for hip mobility.
- **Full snatch and full clean (catch)**: high technical cost, high overhead-shoulder load, and the unique stimulus is in the 2nd pull which a clean pull captures. ROI for a sprint swimmer is poor. Skip unless the athlete already has an O-lifting background.
- **Heavy back squat with a high bar position**: forces a shoulder-loaded position that aggravates anterior capsule in many swimmers. Front squat or trap bar deadlift are usually better. (McEvoy abandoned squatting entirely; see brain — though that decision was about ankle stiffness, not shoulders.)
- **Long (200–400m) tempo runs**: Francis's lower-body tempo runs at 70–75% on grass are GREAT for track sprinters' connective-tissue capillarization. For swimmers, the impact load and time-cost are not justified given the swimmer is already accumulating tissue stimulus from kicking. Substitute Schexnayder GS circuits.
- **Block-start specific drills (3-point start, 4-point start)**: A swim block start is mechanically *similar* to a track start but the timing and arm action diverge enough that direct translation is weak. Use track-start *concepts* (force angles, drive-phase posture) but not the drills themselves.
- **Max-velocity flying sprints (30m fly's at 100% on flat track)**: too injurious for masters swimmers and the specific stimulus (top-speed mechanics with hip-flexor-driven swing) doesn't transfer. Hill sprints capture the CNS demand without the injury cost.
- **Olympic lifts as a "complex" alongside the main session for swimmers without lifting background**: the Yessis 1x20 path is safer. Olympic-derivative work should wait for a clear strength base (e.g., trap bar deadlift 1.5x BW).

## 7. Application to 50m freestyle sprint — concrete coach checklist

A swim S&C coach building a sprint program with track-sprint cross-application would do the following:

1. **Adopt Francis's high-low microcycle.** 3 high days, 3 low days, 1 rest. Eliminate all grey-zone work.
2. **Replace traditional barbell jump squats with trap bar jumps** at 10–20% trap-bar 1RM, 4×3, 1–2x/week.
3. **Add bounding** (alternating, then single-leg) 1–2x/week off-season; 30–120 contacts depending on level. Track distance-per-10-bounds as a power KPI.
4. **Add heavy sled work** (30–40% BW push, 6×10–15m) once weekly off-season as the primary horizontal-force stimulus for block/wall push-off.
5. **Add hill sprints** (6–8×10s on 8% gradient) for masters or detrained athletes — safer entry to CNS work than flat sprinting or in-pool max efforts.
6. **Substitute clean pulls / hang power cleans for full O-lifts.** Skip the catch unless athlete has lifting background. 4×3, 1–2x/week.
7. **Replace recovery-day "easy 2k swims" with Schexnayder GS circuits.** Better tissue stimulus, better mobility, no shoulder volume cost.
8. **Use one contrast/complex pair per power session** off-season (e.g., trap bar DL → trap bar jump). Drop in-season — too CNS-costly.
9. **For deconditioned masters athletes (e.g., 9-month layoff), open with a 6–8 week Yessis 1x20 block.** Builds tissue tolerance with minimal CNS cost; equal sprint outcomes vs. traditional in tested cohorts. Then transition to the high-low model.
10. **Apply the 95% rule to in-pool work.** Time every quality rep. Anything 76–94% gets re-categorized as warm-up (<75%) or as race-pace (>95%); nothing in the middle.
11. **Track CNS with HRV + reaction time + sprint kick-board time.** Don't run a high day if any two are flagged.
12. **Cap heavy O-lift derivatives 4 weeks out from competition.** Maintain trap bar jumps and bounding at low volume; eliminate 10–14 days out (consistent with existing brain taper protocol).

## 8. Honest uncertainty

- **The high-low model has been adapted for swimming** (SimpliFaster's "Applying a High-Low Autoregulation Model for Swimming," 2023) but published RCTs in swimmers are absent. The evidence base is coach-experience + transfer logic, not direct trials.
- **Heavy sled work (>30% BW) for swimmers**: zero published RCTs. Logic is strong (pure horizontal force) but the swim-specific transfer coefficient is unknown.
- **Yessis 1x20 in swimmers**: tested in football and youth athletes (Bracius; North Scott HS data). No published swim trial. Treat as a credible but unverified hypothesis.
- **Bounding GCT targets**: 0.12–0.18s is from track sprinters. Whether the swim-relevant target should be longer (because swim block GCT is longer than track flat-sprint GCT) is unclear.
- **Olympic lift derivatives in masters swimmers (30+) returning from layoff**: no specific data. Standard caution — establish strength base first, derivatives second.
