1. **Lineage is blended, not pure**: Matt Smith's training background is a *fusion* of physical therapy, gymnastics, time with Shaolin monks (mobility/flexibility), and time learning from Charles Poliquin (strength). He is not a PICP-certified Poliquin disciple as far as public record shows. 2. **Smith's "official" entry into McEvoy's program was mobility, not strength**: Per McEvoy's own Instagram (Aug 2024), McEvoy started working with Smith in **early 2023** primarily for shoulder and hip mobility — and credits the work for his 15m dive time dropping from 5.6s (Oct 2022) to **5.16s in the Olympic final** (Paris 2024). The strength role appears to have *expanded* post-Paris, not started there.
3. **Dan Rogers handled strength pre-Paris**; **Matt Smith took over the integrated strength + mobility role post-Paris (mid-2024 onward)** — this is what the brain already has, and is consistent with the public record.
4. **Verifiable McEvoy gym session (~6 wk pre-WR)** has concrete numbers: trap bar deadlift 165 kg × 2 (cluster format), bench press 85 kg × 6, weighted pull-ups 70 kg × 2, bench pull 105 kg × 6 — clearly **low-rep, high-load, posterior-chain-and-pull dominant**. Tempos not publicly disclosed in detail; Smith is a tempo-aware coach but the rep schemes shown are explosive/cluster, not classic Poliquin GVT.
5. **Poliquin's signature methods Smith likely draws from**: structural-balance assessment (find weak link, fix it), tempo prescriptions, cluster sets, post-tetanic potentiation, and — for mobility — Poliquin's emphasis on full-ROM strength rather than range-then-strength as separate disciplines.
6. **What's *not* Poliquin in Smith's McEvoy work**: GVT (10×10 hypertrophy) is *absent*. The McEvoy program is *low-volume, high-intensity, neural*, the opposite end of Poliquin's spectrum. Smith has selected from the Poliquin toolkit, not adopted the canon.
7. **Biggest takeaway for swim S&C**: a Poliquin-influenced coach offers what traditional swim dryland often lacks — **structural balance assessment**, **tempo control**, **mobility-as-strength** (loaded full ROM, not stretch-then-lift), and **neural/cluster methods**. None of this requires removing barbell squats; that was a McEvoy-specific call (ankle stiffness for kick).

## 3. Charles Poliquin — concise primer of what Smith inherits

Poliquin (1961–2018) was a Canadian strength coach who built ~800 Olympic-level athletes' programs across more than 20 sports. He was prolific, opinionated, and divisive. The methodological pillars that swim coaches should know:

### 3.1 Structural Balance

**The idea**: muscles are evaluated as ratios, not absolutes. If antagonist muscles are weak relative to prime movers, the brain throttles output (CNS protective inhibition). The *fastest* way past a strength plateau is often training the lagging antagonist.

**Canonical upper-body ratios (close-grip bench = 100%)**:

| Lift | Ratio |
|------|-------|
| Close-grip bench press 1RM | 100% |
| Incline bench press | 91% |
| Parallel bar dip (BW + load) | 117% |
| Supinated chin-up 1RM | 87% |
| Lying triceps extension | 40% |
| Seated DB external rotation 8RM | ~10% |
| Bent-over DB Trap-3 raise 8RM | ~10% |

**For swim**: external rotation strength at ~10% of close-grip bench is the diagnostic for shoulder health. If a swimmer benches 100 kg and can't externally rotate 10 kg × 8, they're a swimmer's-shoulder candidate. This single test alone justifies a Poliquin-style assessment for swim teams. (The Smith → McEvoy program almost certainly tests something like this; not publicly disclosed.)

### 3.2 Tempo Prescriptions (4-digit code)

Format: **[eccentric] [bottom pause] [concentric] [top pause]**. Example: 4010 = 4s lowering, no pause, 1s lifting, no pause. "X" in the concentric position = explosive intent.

**Why it matters for sprinters**: tempo controls **time under tension**, which dictates whether a set develops:
- **Neural strength** (1-1X1 or 2-0-X-0, low TUT, heavy loads) — what sprinters need
- **Functional hypertrophy** (3-1-1-0, moderate TUT) — adding swim-relevant muscle
- **Sarcoplasmic hypertrophy** (4-0-2-0, classic GVT, high TUT) — bodybuilding, *not* what sprinters need

Most generic gym programs prescribe sets and reps but not tempo. A Poliquin-style program *always* prescribes tempo, because two athletes doing 5×5 at 80% with different tempos get fundamentally different adaptations.

### 3.3 German Volume Training (GVT, 10×10 @ ~60% 1RM, 4-0-2-0 tempo)

Poliquin's most famous method, popularized in 1996. Designed to "shock" hypertrophy. **Not relevant to McEvoy** and probably not in Smith's swim toolkit — it's a body-comp / off-season hypertrophy method. Mentioned here so coaches don't conflate "Poliquin lineage" with "GVT for swimmers" (which would be a poor fit).

### 3.4 Cluster Sets

Instead of 5×5 straight, do 5 sets of (1+1+1+1+1) with 10–20s intra-set rest. Each rep is fresher → higher intra-set load → more total neural drive at heavy loads. Poliquin: "The best method to get stronger is clusters."

**McEvoy's session uses cluster patterns** (e.g. 4 rounds: 2× pull-up @70 kg + 4× Keiser pulldown @330 lb). The "2× then potentiate-target" structure echoes Poliquin's potentiation logic.

### 3.5 Post-Tetanic / Post-Activation Potentiation (PTP / PAP)

A high-force action briefly increases the muscle's capacity to recruit fibers and fire fast. Poliquin's "1-6 method" pairs a heavy single (e.g. 1RM) with a high-rep set (e.g. 6) — the heavy rep potentiates the higher-rep work. Modern French Contrast Method (heavy → plyometric → assisted plyometric → over-speed) is the same logic.

**McEvoy's program already uses French Contrast** (existing brain content). Smith's involvement here may be in **how the heavy lift is loaded and tempo'd** so it potentiates without over-fatiguing.

### 3.6 Training-Age Periodization

Poliquin emphasized that **training age** (years lifting) > biological age. Beginners need motor control and frequency; intermediates need accumulation/intensification cycles; advanced athletes need precise variation and CNS management. McEvoy at age ~31 with 10+ years of training is squarely in the "advanced" bucket — which is exactly why his program is heavy/low-volume/neural.

### 3.7 BioSignature — *do not import this*

Poliquin's BioSignature program claimed to map skinfold body-fat distribution to specific hormonal imbalances (e.g. high quadricep fat = low androgens). It was widely criticized; the science doesn't hold. **Structural balance ≠ BioSignature.** Structural balance is musculoskeletal (defensible). BioSignature is endocrinological (not). When evaluating a Poliquin-influenced coach, distinguish these. Smith's public output focuses on movement and strength, with no BioSignature content visible.

## 5. The McEvoy program — extending the brain with Smith's verifiable specifics

The single most useful primary source is the publicly published "1 week of Olympic Champion Cam McEvoy's training" breakdown (theswimsuitguy.com), which shows real loads. Cross-referenced with the YouTube gym-session video already in brain (DME7vuLuTSg), here's the verifiable strength frame:

### 5.1 Weekly gym structure (3 sessions, A/B/A or A/B/A pattern)

**Session A (Tuesday, Saturday) — pull-dominant**
1. **Warm-up pull-ups**: BW × 5, +25 kg × 5, +50 kg × 3
2. **Strength cluster**: 4 rounds of [+70 kg × 2 weighted pull-up → 4 × Keiser pulldown @ 330–450 lb]
3. **Hypertrophy/shoulder-health block**: 3 rounds of [+50 kg × 6 pull-up → 25 kg × 10 reverse cable fly → 25 kg × 10 tricep cable extension/arm]
4. **Posterior chain accessory**: 3 × 14 lying hamstring curls @ 35 kg

**Session B (Thursday) — hinge + press hybrid (with pool integration)**
1. Keiser pulldown warm-up
2. **Pull-up potentiation + dive**: 6 rounds of [+70 kg × 2 pull-up → 1 dive 25] (every 10 min)
3. **Trap bar deadlift cluster + jump**: 4 rounds of [165 kg × 2 trap bar DL → 3-count movement jumps]
4. **Press/pull pair**: 3 rounds of [105 kg × 6 bench pull → 85 kg × 6 bench press]

### 5.2 What this tells us about Smith's prescription style

- **Cluster + contrast structure dominates** (heavy double + dynamic/explosive complement). This is Poliquin-PAP logic and overlaps with French Contrast.
- **Loads are very heavy, reps very low** (mostly 2–6). Neural intent. Far from GVT. Far from traditional swim-dryland 8–12 rep ranges.
- **Volume per session is modest** — a swimmer with traditional dryland would do 2–3× this volume. The McEvoy session is *neural CNS work*, not metabolic.
- **Pull > Push by a wide margin**. Pull-ups, bench pulls, pulldowns dominate. Bench press appears once per week at 85 kg × 6. This *correlates with* Poliquin structural-balance logic (chin-up should be ~87% of CG bench; over-developing pulling balances swim-induced anterior dominance).
- **Posterior chain via hinge, not squat**. Trap bar from the floor (not Romanian) at 165 kg × 2. This is hip-extension power without ankle-mobility demand. The brain already documents the *reason* (ankle stiffness for kick) — Smith's framing is consistent with mobility-aware S&C: *don't force a movement an athlete's structure won't allow; pick an equivalent that hits the same chain*.
- **No olympic lifts, no plyometrics in this gym day** — plyo lives elsewhere (or is integrated as the "movement jump" piece). McEvoy's plyo mostly happens via climbing/bouldering and explosive jumps within the lifting structure.
- **Tempos not disclosed** in published sources. From the structure (heavy doubles, dynamic finishes), educated inference: **neural tempo, likely 2-0-X-0 or 1-1-X-1** for the heavy doubles; controlled-eccentric for accessory rows and curls. *This is inference, not Smith's word.*

### 5.3 Why the squat was removed (extending what's in brain)

The brain has: "ankle stiffness → squat eliminated → trap bar from the floor." Smith's framing of *why* a coach makes that swap is implicit in his published philosophy:

- "Foundation building" before pattern complexity — if the ankle won't allow a deep squat without compensating, don't force it.
- Smith's mobility audit identifies the limiter; rather than train *around* the ankle (which a generic S&C coach might do), he trains *the chain that doesn't require it* (hinge from floor) and *separately* trains the ankle in mobility work.
- This is a clean Poliquin-influenced principle: **find the weak link, fix it, train what works in the meantime**. It's structural balance applied to ROM, not just muscle ratios.

### 5.4 Why bench press was added (extending what's in brain)

McEvoy avoided bench his whole career, then added it post-Japan-Open 2025 (per primary-source extraction in brain). Translation through Smith's likely lens:

- McEvoy's pull volume was already high (climbing + lifting + swim-pull). His push:pull ratio was *severely* anterior-deficient at the *anti-glenohumeral* level (the dip/bench/horizontal-press chain).
- Adding bench *restores structural balance* — not because swimmers need to bench heavy, but because chronic pull-dominance without push counterweight produces forward-rounded shoulders and weak triceps that bottleneck the late-pull phase.
- **The dose is low** (1×/wk, 6 reps @ 85 kg) — just enough to maintain the antagonist, not enough to bias hypertrophy. This is textbook structural-balance dosing.

### 5.5 Mobility integration

This is Smith's actual specialty and where the McEvoy program likely diverges *most* from generic Poliquin. Verifiable points:

- McEvoy's 15m dive time dropped 5.6s → 5.16s (Oct 2022 → Olympic final), and McEvoy attributes it primarily to mobility work with Smith (shoulder + hip ROM) — a 0.44s gain, which in 50m terms is enormous because dive contributes ~20.5% of race time and mobility-limited dive shape is a hard ceiling.
- Smith's principle: train *strength through full ROM*, not "stretch then lift." Loaded mobility — overhead work at end-range, hip mobility under load — is the bridge.
- Volume principle: 3–5 targeted stretches, 1–2x/wk per skill. Mobility is *not* daily slog. It's targeted, assessed, reassessed.

This is genuinely novel for swim S&C, and it's the strongest case for hiring a Poliquin-influenced *mobility-trained* coach over a generic strength coach.

## 7. Translatable principles — what any swim coach can extract

Even if you reject the McEvoy paradigm wholesale, these Poliquin-via-Smith principles are independently defensible:

1. **Run a structural-balance audit once per season**. Test close-grip bench, weighted chin, seated DB external rotation 8RM, bent-over Trap-3 raise 8RM. Compare to Poliquin ratios. Address the worst gap first.
2. **Prescribe tempo, not just sets and reps**. The same 5×5 at 80% is a different stimulus at 4-0-2-0 vs. 2-0-X-0. Pick deliberately.
3. **Use cluster sets when load matters more than volume**. Sprint-relevant phases benefit more from 5×(2+2+2+2) at 90% than 5×8 at 75%.
4. **Train mobility under load** — overhead with weight, hip openers under tempo, end-range isometrics. Stretching alone doesn't transfer to streamline because the swimmer can't stabilize there under speed.
5. **Identify the weakest link and fix it explicitly** — don't just program around it. Build a separate block to rehab the limiter.
6. **Push:pull ratio matters**. Swim biases pull volume; counterweight with deliberate (low-dose) push to keep shoulders healthy.
7. **Pick the lift that hits the chain without aggravating the limiter**. Squat → trap bar if ankle won't cooperate. Bench → dip if anterior shoulder is irritated. Conventional DL → trap bar if back is fragile. Same chain, different vehicle.

## 8. Skeptical section — what's hype, what's underexplored

1. **Survivorship bias is huge here.** McEvoy is one athlete with one WR. Smith's *other* swim clients are not public. Tim Lane's juniors using the method are reportedly improving but no public data. The method-result link is plausible but not robust.
2. **Smith's marketing copy is louder than his teaching content.** The "lived with Shaolin monks + worked with Poliquin" framing is repeated verbatim across course-resale sites (which simply mirror his own copy). It signals brand more than verifiable curriculum. He doesn't appear to have peer-reviewed publications, conference talks, or a written program library.
3. **The Poliquin name itself is contested.** Poliquin's structural balance and tempo work are defensible. His BioSignature was pseudoscience. His personal style was abrasive and cult-of-personality. Smith inheriting "Poliquin influence" is positive only if he inherited the methodologically defensible parts. The fact that his McEvoy program shows no BioSignature signature suggests he did filter — but this can't be confirmed without his curriculum.
4. **The "mobility caused the dive time drop" attribution is McEvoy's own story.** It's plausible. But dive time also drops from technique, plyometric base, body composition, equipment (suit), confidence, and training freshness. McEvoy attributing it cleanly to mobility is a single causal narrative imposed on a multi-causal outcome. Don't over-update on it.
5. **What didn't work?** We don't know. McEvoy talks about the McEvoy method; we don't hear about athletes for whom it failed. Smith's mobility clients are public; his swim-strength clients (other than McEvoy) are not. Absent failure cases, the success rate of the protocol is unknowable.
6. **Is the strength program even "Poliquin-style" in any deep sense, or is it just heavy-low-rep training that happens to share vocabulary?** A coach without any Poliquin exposure could write a similar McEvoy session by independently arriving at: low reps, heavy load, cluster structure, contrast pairs. The Poliquin signature would be in *tempo prescription* and *structural balance auditing* — neither of which is publicly disclosed in McEvoy's program. So the claim "Poliquin lineage caused the WR" is unfalsifiable on current evidence.

## 9. Application to 50m freestyle sprint — concrete principles a coach could test

For an athlete (Toey or any sprinter) who wants to *try* the Smith/Poliquin direction without a $$ coach contract, these are the testable, low-risk experiments:

1. **Do a structural balance audit (1 session)**. Test CG bench 1RM, weighted chin 1RM, DB external rotation 8RM/arm, Trap-3 raise 8RM. Compute ratios. The worst deficit becomes the priority of the next 4-week block.
2. **Add tempo prescription to existing strength work**. Pick one variable per phase: heavy phase = 2-0-X-0; functional hypertrophy = 3-0-1-0; eccentric phase = 4-0-1-0. Don't change everything; change one thing and reassess.
3. **Replace one straight-set pattern with a cluster** for 4 weeks. e.g. trap bar 5×3 @ 85% → 5×(1+1+1) @ 92.5% with 15s intra-set rest. Track 1RM at the end.
4. **Run a 4-week loaded-mobility block before the heavy block.** Overhead carries, deep ATG split squats with pause, end-range hip CARs under load. Re-test streamline ROM after 4 weeks.
5. **If you have ankle stiffness limiting squat depth, swap to trap bar from blocks (not floor) for 4 weeks** while you separately train ankle dorsiflexion. Then re-test squat. (Don't drop squat permanently without evidence the trap bar variant is delivering equal or better starts/turns.)
6. **Don't import GVT for a sprint cycle.** GVT is a hypertrophy / off-season body-comp tool. It's the *least* sprint-relevant Poliquin method.
7. **Don't import BioSignature.** Skinfold-to-hormone mapping is not science. If a "Poliquin-trained coach" pitches it, that's a red flag.

## 10. Bottom line for the brain

- The "Matt Smith is a Poliquin lineage coach" claim in the McEvoy doc is **directionally correct** but should be softened to "Poliquin-influenced, plus Shaolin / gymnastics / PT background — with mobility as his core specialty."
- Smith's most original contribution to McEvoy's program is **mobility**, not strength. The strength program is competent and Poliquin-flavored but not radically novel; the mobility program is what differentiates Smith from a generic S&C coach.
- For Toey's 50m freestyle pursuit, the **highest-leverage Poliquin/Smith ideas to import** are: (1) structural balance audit, (2) tempo-prescribed strength, (3) loaded mobility for streamline/dive ROM, (4) cluster sets for heavy phases. **Lowest-leverage / skip**: GVT, BioSignature, the cult-of-personality vibe.
- The McEvoy program is *one path* through a sprint-strength problem space; not "the answer." Verify on your own body before adopting wholesale.
