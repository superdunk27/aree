## 1. Executive Summary — What the RCTs Actually Show

Seven non-obvious findings up front:

1. **The "2-3% in 6-8 weeks" headline is real but smaller than swimmers think.** In the gold-standard RCT (Wang et al., 2025, *J Sports Sci Med*, n=27 university swimmers), 6 weeks of plyometrics improved 50m freestyle by **1.8%** (Cohen's d = 0.254, "small") vs +0.7% in the endurance control. Maximal strength training (MST) won outright at **2.4%** (d = 0.418). Plyo alone is rarely the biggest mover — but it is the biggest **start** mover.
2. **Start time (to 15 m) responds bigger than swim time.** Same Wang RCT: PT improved 15 m start by **>3.6%** (d > 0.41) vs >2.0% in control. Bishop et al. (2009, *J Strength Cond Res*) found PT cut 5.5 m time by **−0.59 s** vs −0.21 s in habitual-only group (p < 0.01). This is where plyo earns its keep.
3. **Horizontal jumps beat vertical jumps for swim start specificity** — but the evidence is wobbly. Rebutini et al. (2014, 2016) showed +10.4% takeoff velocity and +22% horizontal velocity at water entry after 9 weeks of progressive long-jump-loaded plyo. Yet a direct head-to-head pilot (Pushing Up vs Pushing Out, *PeerJ* 2021, n=11) found **no significant between-group difference** — likely underpowered.
4. **Effective dose is modest: 2 sessions/week × 60-110 ground contacts × 6-8 weeks.** Saez de Villarreal et al. meta-analyses converge on ~80-100 contacts/week being as good as 160-200 (no extra gain from more volume; just more soreness). Low/moderate frequency (2/wk) beats high (4/wk) on jump and sprint outcomes (de Villarreal 2008, *JSCR*).
5. **Strength prerequisite matters.** Plyo on a weak base produces small gains and elevated injury risk. Athletes should hit a back squat ≥1.5× bodyweight (or trap-bar DL ≥1.75× BW) AND demonstrate sound landing mechanics before depth jumps. RSI (Reactive Strength Index) ≥1.5 is a working entry threshold.
6. **The block start is a near-isometric/concentric event, NOT a true SSC** — most kick-start research notes there is no rapid eccentric pre-load on the block. Plyo transfers via *neural drive and tendon stiffness* adaptations more than via "training the SSC of the start itself." This is widely misunderstood (see McMahon et al. 2021).
7. **Turn and underwater dolphin kick plyo evidence is THIN.** No RCT has isolated plyo's effect on turn time or underwater fly-kick velocity. Best inference: hip-driven dolphin kick benefits from rapid-SSC posterior-chain plyo (hip pop drills, snap-down to broad jump, kettlebell swings as quasi-plyo) — but this is mechanistic reasoning, not RCT evidence. Be honest about this.

## 2. Mechanism — SSC and Why It Matters (and Where It Doesn't)

The **stretch-shortening cycle (SSC)** is a coordinated eccentric → amortization (transition) → concentric muscle action that produces more force/power than a pure concentric (Komi 2000; Turner & Jeffreys 2010, *Strength Cond J*). Mechanisms:

- **Stored elastic energy** in tendon and series elastic component (recoil during concentric)
- **Stretch reflex** (Ia afferent → α-motoneuron facilitation)
- **Pre-activation** of muscle prior to ground contact (raises stiffness, lowers EMD)
- **Active state** persisting from eccentric → concentric (faster cross-bridge cycling)

**Short SSC** (<250 ms ground contact, e.g., depth jump rebound, sprint footstrike) relies more on **elastic recoil + reflex**. **Long SSC** (>250 ms, e.g., countermovement jump, broad jump from pause) relies more on **active state + voluntary drive**. Different exercises train different SSC durations — and a sprint swimmer needs both.

### Where SSC applies in a 50 m race
| Phase | SSC type | Evidence quality |
|-------|----------|------------------|
| Block start (kick start) | Mostly **concentric** from semi-isometric set position; rear leg has slight eccentric load | Strong: this is NOT a classic SSC event |
| Wall turn / push-off | **Long SSC** (knee/hip eccentric brake → concentric push) | Moderate — biomechanically clear, RCT evidence thin |
| Underwater dolphin kick | **Short SSC** at hip flexors/extensors and core, ~0.3-0.5 s cycle | Mechanistic only; no plyo RCTs isolating this |
| Stroke catch | **Long SSC** (mild) at lat/teres on hand entry | Weak; mostly conjecture |

The implication is that plyo for sprint swimming earns its biggest direct transfer at the **turn**, indirect transfer at the **start** (via tendon stiffness + RFD), and *speculative* transfer at the **underwater kick**. The brain should not pretend otherwise.

## 3. Plyometric Taxonomy — Concrete Examples for Swimmers

Categories are from Chu & Myer (2013) and Saez de Villarreal et al. reviews:

**Tier 1 — Jumps in place (entry level, 80-150 contacts OK)**
- Pogo hops, ankle hops, tuck jumps, line hops
- GCT goal: 100-180 ms; primarily Achilles + calf stiffness
- Use: warm-up, RSI building, week 1-2 of any block

**Tier 2 — Standing jumps (neuro-mechanical foundation)**
- Squat jump (no countermovement), countermovement jump (CMJ), broad jump, lateral bound
- 3-5 reps × 3-5 sets, full recovery (1-3 min)
- Use: weeks 1-8; track CMJ height + broad jump distance

**Tier 3 — Multiple hops/bounds (long SSC)**
- Repeated broad jumps, bounding for distance, single-leg hops 3-5 reps
- 60-80 contacts per session
- Use: weeks 3-10; lateral asymmetry diagnostic

**Tier 4 — Depth jumps (short SSC, advanced)**
- Drop from 30-60 cm box → land → reactive jump
- Above 60 cm GCT lengthens and RSI drops (Walsh et al. research line)
- 3-5 reps × 3-4 sets, full rest (3+ min)
- Use: weeks 4-8 only after Tier 1-3 mastered + back squat ≥1.5× BW
- **Box height heuristic**: pick the tallest box at which GCT stays <250 ms and jump height does NOT decrease relative to a 30 cm baseline. This is "optimal drop height" — usually 30-45 cm for masters, 45-60 cm for elite young athletes.

**Tier 5 — Sport-specific reactive plyo**
- Block-start drills (mock kick start with progressive intensity)
- Wall push-off with med-ball throw at exit
- "Start jumps" (Pushing Up/Pushing Out 2021): broad jump from a swim-start stance with rear leg loaded

**Upper-body plyo (medicine ball)**
- 3-6 kg ball: chest pass, overhead slam, rotational throw
- Seated MB throw correlates moderately with sprint swim performance (r ≈ 0.4-0.6 in University of Alicante data)
- 3 sets × 6-8 throws, 1-2×/week

## 4. Evidence Base — Swim-Start RCTs With Effect Sizes

| Study | n / age | Protocol | Duration | Outcome | Effect |
|-------|---------|----------|----------|---------|--------|
| **Bishop et al. 2009** *JSCR* | 22 / 12-13 yr | 2× 60-min plyo sessions/wk added to habitual swim | 8 wk | Time to 5.5 m | **−0.59 s** vs −0.21 s control (p < 0.01) |
| | | | | Takeoff velocity | +0.19 m/s vs −0.07 m/s control (p < 0.01) |
| **Potdevin et al. 2011** *JSCR* | ~23 / 14 yr (pubescent) | Long, lateral, high & depth jumps 2×/wk before swim | 6 wk | 50 m crawl with dive | Significant ↑ avg speed (PG vs CG, p<0.05); +5-7% range typical |
| | | | | Squat jump, CMJ height | Significant ↑ in plyo group |
| **Rebutini et al. 2014** *JSCR* (Plyometric Long Jump w/ Progressive Loading) | 10 swimmers | Progressive-loaded long jumps | 9 wk | Takeoff velocity | **+10.4%** |
| **Rebutini et al. 2016** | 10 | Same line | 9 wk | Horizontal force at block | +7%; impulse +9%; horizontal entry velocity **+22%**; CoM displacement +5% |
| | | | | Hip peak torque / RTD | +47% / **+108%** (very large) |
| **Wang et al. 2025** *J Sports Sci Med* (PMC11877300) | 27 / ~20 yr (Tier 2) | 2 sessions/wk: 64-108 ground contacts (jumps + MB throws + explosive push-ups) | 6 wk | 50 m freestyle | **+1.8%** (d = 0.254) vs +0.7% control |
| | | | | 15 m start time | **>+3.6%** (d > 0.406) vs +2.0% control |
| **García-Ramos et al. 2016** (descriptive, not RCT) | 38 national-level | n/a | n/a | Predictors of start | Horizontal acceleration & horizontal takeoff velocity = strongest predictors of 15 m time |
| **"Pushing Up vs Pushing Out" pilot 2021** *PeerJ* | 11 / ~21 yr | HF (hip thrust + prowler + start jump) vs VF (squat + RFE split + squat jump), 8 wk × 2/wk | 8 wk | 5 m, 15 m start | **No sig between-group difference** (likely underpowered + concurrent swim volume confound) |

**Read this table critically:**
- Bishop 2009 has the cleanest design but on **adolescents** (puberty confounds). Effect on 5.5 m is largest in literature but partly maturation.
- Rebutini studies are *small* (n=10) and lack control groups — high bias risk; large effects may be overstated.
- Wang 2025 is the highest-quality RCT to date (3-arm, controlled, university-aged) and gives the most defensible numbers: **plyo ≈ +1.8% in 50 m, ~+3.6% in start.** Lower than Bishop but more honest.
- The Pushing Up/Pushing Out 2021 null result is a humility check: in already-trained collegiate swimmers with high pool volume, isolated direction-specific lower-body work for 8 weeks may not move the needle by itself.

## 5. Programming Variables

**Frequency**: 2 sessions/wk for 6-10 weeks is the meta-analytic sweet spot (de Villarreal et al. 2008, *JSCR*; Sáez de Villarreal et al. 2010, *J Strength Cond Res* meta). 4×/wk does not produce additional gains and increases injury risk.

**Volume**: 80-100 contacts/wk = 160-200 contacts/wk on most jump outcomes. Start at ~60 contacts/session, progress to ~120 over 6 weeks.

**Intensity progression** (swimmer-friendly 8-week template):
| Week | Tier focus | Contacts | Box / load |
|------|-----------|----------|-----------|
| 1-2 | Tier 1-2 (pogo, CMJ, broad jump) | 60-80 | ground only |
| 3-4 | + Tier 3 (bounds, lateral) | 80-100 | ground only |
| 5-6 | + Tier 4 light depth | 100-120 | 30 cm box |
| 7 | + Tier 4 progressed + Tier 5 start jumps | 110 | 45 cm box |
| 8 | Deload before pool taper or test | 60 | 30 cm box |

**Rest**: 2-3 min between Tier 4 sets; 1-2 min between Tier 1-2 sets. Quality over fatigue — every contact should be max-effort. If jump height drops >10% within a set, terminate.

**Timing within session/day**: First in dryland session (CNS fresh). At minimum 6 hours separation from heavy strength; 24-48 hours from race-pace pool.

**Contrast / complex pairings**: Heavy lift (e.g., trap-bar DL @ 85% × 3) → 90 s rest → ballistic jump (broad jump × 3). Evidence for swim start PAP is *moderate* (Sarramian et al. 2015 and the Nature 2022 study found PAP warm-ups equally effective to standard race warm-up — useful tool, not magical). Use 3-5 minutes between heavy and ballistic for true PAP window in trained athletes.

## 6. Prerequisites & Contraindications

**Required before depth jumps / Tier 4:**
- Back squat ≥1.5× BW or trap-bar DL ≥1.75× BW (tendon-loading floor)
- Demonstrated soft, balanced landing on broad jump (knee tracking, hip-dominant)
- RSI ≥1.5 on a 30 cm drop test
- No active patellar or Achilles tendinopathy symptoms (VISA-P/A above functional cutoff)
- Adequate ankle dorsiflexion (knee-to-wall ≥10 cm) — meta-analytic risk factor for patellar tendinopathy

**Contraindications / red flags (skip plyo or modify):**
- Untrained to resistance training
- Severe arthritis, osteoporosis, recent lower-limb injury
- BMI substantially elevated (force × bodyweight risks)
- Acute Achilles or patellar tendon pain (>3/10 NRS during jumping)
- Inadequate sleep / readiness (HRV crash) — plyo on under-recovered CNS = wasted session + injury risk

**Evidence-based age modifiers (relevant for Toey, age 31):**
A 2020 systematic review (Vetrovsky et al. *Sports Medicine - Open*, PMC7447006) showed age-adapted plyo improves jump performance and dynamic strength in older men similarly or more than traditional resistance training. Practical implications:
- Box heights for masters: cap at 20-30 cm initially; build to 45 cm if pain-free
- Cap volume lower (start 50-60 contacts/session vs 80 for college athletes)
- Add 1 extra recovery day between sessions (so 2/wk = Mon/Thu, not Mon/Wed)
- Pre-screen: ECG recommended >40 yr; for 30s, skip unless cardiac history
- Calf-injury caution on unilateral plyo (cited risk in masters)

## 7. Risk Management — Achilles, Patellar, Low Back

**Tendon load math.** Plyometrics require <250 ms GCT to qualify as plyo (Davies et al. 2015, IJSPT). Ground reaction forces during depth jumps reach **3-7× BW** at the Achilles and **5-9× BW** at the patellar tendon. Programming error is the #1 cause of non-contact tendinopathy in jumping sports.

**Volume guardrails:**
- ≤80 contacts/session for Tier 4 work
- ≤200 contacts/week total
- Modifiable risk factors per meta (Sprague et al. 2018): high CMJ, high body weight, low ankle DF — manage all three

**Deload signals** (any one = reduce volume 40-50% next session):
- Achilles morning stiffness >15 min
- Patellar tendon pain >2/10 during single-leg decline squat
- Jump height drop >10% session-on-session at fixed volume
- HRV drop >7% baseline for 2+ days

**Tendon prep** (do this BEFORE plyo block):
- Heavy slow-resistance for soleus/gastroc (tempo calf raises 3×8 @ 70% 1RM, 3 s eccentric)
- Spanish squats or single-leg decline squats for patellar tendon
- 4-6 weeks of this during the strength phase reduces in-block tendinopathy risk substantially (Beyer et al. 2015 line of work on heavy-slow-resistance for tendons)

**Low-back risk**: Med-ball slams + repeated bounding on a fatigued lumbar spine = trouble. Cap MB slams to 3 sets × 6-8; do NOT chain into deadlifts the same day.

## 8. Application to a 50 m Freestyle Sprint Block

**Toey's context** (per existing brain): 31 yr, masters sprinter, 50 m PB 24.16, 9 months detrained. The right plyo dose for his case is **conservative, ramp-up oriented, and fully integrated with the rebuild of strength base**.

### Phase 0 — Tendon Prep (weeks 1-4 of return)
- NO plyo yet. Heavy slow-resistance calf, soleus, patellar prep.
- Strength build: trap-bar DL, hip thrust, bench, OHP
- Optional: pogo hops 2×20 as motor pattern, low intensity

### Phase 1 — Plyo Introduction (weeks 5-8)
- 2×/wk: Tier 1 (pogo, line hops) + Tier 2 (CMJ, broad jump)
- 50-70 contacts per session
- Goal: re-acclimate tendons, baseline RSI, broad jump distance test

### Phase 2 — Plyo Build (weeks 9-12)
- 2×/wk: Tier 1 warm-up + Tier 2 main + introduce Tier 3 bounds
- 80-100 contacts per session
- Add 1×/wk Tier 4 light (30 cm depth jump 3×3) if pain-free

### Phase 3 — Specific (weeks 13-16, lead-in to peak)
- 1-2×/wk: Tier 4 progressed (45 cm) + Tier 5 sport-specific start jumps + MB throws
- 80-100 contacts
- Contrast pairings 1×/wk (trap-bar DL → broad jump)
- 2×/wk swim block-start practice with feedback

### Taper (last 10-14 days)
- Drop volume 50-60%; keep intensity
- 1 light session 3-5 days out: Tier 1 + 1 broad jump + 1 MB throw
- 0 plyo in final 48 hours

**Expected magnitude**: Realistic gain on 50 m time from this block = **+0.5 to +1.5%** (≈0.12-0.36 s off 24.16). Most of the gain shows up at start + first 15 m. Bigger gains require concurrent strength + technique work — plyo alone is not the lever.

## 9. Honest Gaps — Where Evidence Is Thin

1. **Turn time**: zero RCTs isolate plyo effect on turn split. Best mechanistic case is for long-SSC depth jumps mimicking the wall push-off. We're inferring, not measuring.
2. **Underwater dolphin kick velocity**: zero RCTs. Mechanistic case: hip-flexor/extensor short-SSC drills (hanging knee snaps, kettlebell swings, snap-down to broad jump) MAY help. Russian twists, MB rotational slams, and hanging leg raises are *core* exercises, not plyo — they don't qualify and shouldn't be sold as plyo for the kick.
3. **Masters-specific dosing for swimmers age 30+**: extrapolated from general masters athlete literature. No swim-specific RCT in this age band.
4. **Sex differences in plyo response in swimmers**: female-specific meta-analyses exist for soccer/team sports (Ozbar 2014; *Frontiers* 2025) but swimmer-specific evidence is sparse.
5. **Optimal contrast pairings for swim start**: PAP for swim start has 1-3 studies showing equivalence to standard warm-up — not superiority. Don't oversell.
6. **Long-term (>12 weeks) trajectory**: most studies are 6-9 weeks. Plateaus, periodization, and detraining curves for plyo in swimmers are largely uncharted.

## Closing Note from Aree

The basic plyo content already in the strength-dryland doc is correct in direction but optimistic in magnitude. The honest evidence-graded picture: **plyo earns ~1.5-3% on the start, ~1-2% on overall 50 m time, only when stacked on a real strength base, and only with disciplined volume.** For Toey specifically — coming back from 9 months detrained at age 31 — the correct path is **strength first, tendon prep second, plyo introduced in week 5+ at conservative volumes, and progress to depth jumps only after demonstrating RSI ≥1.5 and pain-free landings.** The biggest gains in his current state come from rebuilding the strength base (Wang et al. effect size for MST > PT in young trained swimmers); plyo is the polish, not the foundation.
