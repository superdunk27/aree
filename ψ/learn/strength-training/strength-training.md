## Cross-cutting synthesis

### What track sprint S&C, F-V profiling, plyometrics, tendon work, and Poliquin-lineage methods all agree on

1. **Sprint events are won by RFD, not max strength** — and the contact windows in a 50m race (block ~250-400ms, walls ~250-350ms, kick cycles 0.4-0.5s) all sit on the *rising limb* of the force-time curve, where peak strength never gets expressed. Train RFD directly, not via "more 1RM."

2. **The CNS recovers slowly from real high-intensity work** — Charlie Francis's High-Low model and Schexnayder's recovery management converge on this. Poliquin's structural-balance + tempo work fits between the high-CNS days. Most swim programs over-saturate the grey zone (75-95%) and under-deliver true high-quality work.

3. **Mobility ≠ joint stiffness, and joint stiffness ≠ tendon stiffness** — three different mechanical properties that swim coaches conflate. McEvoy's "ankle stiffness" was a passive ROM problem (kept him out of the squat), not a tendon problem. Stiff tendon is a goal; stiff joint usually isn't.

4. **Profile-driven prescription beats template-driven** — F-V profiling, structural balance assessment, RSI testing all point the same direction: figure out what THIS athlete is missing, then close that specific gap. Generic "strength + plyo + Olympic lift" templates leak performance.

5. **Volume is not the lever; intent is** — plyo: ~80-100 contacts/week is as good as 200; strength: 3-6 reps at 80-95% beats 4×10 hypertrophy work for sprint; isometric: 12 minutes of total tendon-loading time per week is the dose. Swim coaches who add volume to "fix" stalled progress are usually digging deeper holes.

### Where the 5 docs disagree (or expose tensions)

- **Plyo vs max strength priority** — Wang 2025 RCT puts max strength ahead of plyo for raw 50m time; Bishop 2009 puts plyo ahead for *start* time. Both are right; the prescription depends on which phase the swimmer is leaking time in.
- **McEvoy's results as evidence** — McEvoy's WR is the most-cited swim sprint case study in the brain, but it's n=1. The Smith/Poliquin lineage doc is appropriately skeptical: the program is competently built, but "Poliquin lineage caused the WR" is unfalsifiable.
- **In-pool F-V** — methodologically excellent, but no RCT yet shows that *correcting* an in-pool F-V imbalance produces a faster 50m. Treat as monitoring tool, not closed-loop prescription engine.

## Honest gaps (where Round 2 might be useful)

Each agent flagged sources where evidence was weak. Aggregated:

1. **Masters / age 30+ specific data** — almost all the sprint S&C literature is on 18-25 year-olds. Programming adjustments for the 30+ athlete returning from layoff are mostly extrapolated from football/track masters work, not swim.
2. **Turn-time and underwater dolphin kick plyo evidence** — zero RCTs isolating these. Mechanistic reasoning only.
3. **Lat/elbow tendon stiffness for the catch** — theoretical; no in-water studies.
4. **Smith's actual training program** — paywalled in SwimSwam; secondary sources have load numbers but no tempo prescriptions.
5. **Optimal RSI target for swim sprint** — extrapolated from track sprint (>2.5 estimated). Not validated for swimming.
6. **Sample 1x20 / Yessis program in masters swimmers returning from layoff** — tested in football/youth, not swim.

Round 2 candidates: longitudinal masters athlete adaptation, in-water tendon/RFD measurement, deep dive on Smith's actual session structure (if/when SwimSwam paywall accessible), Yessis 1x20 application case study.
