# 01 — Dryland → Swim Sprint Transfer

**Question**: Does dryland strength training translate into faster swim times, and through what mechanism?

---

## Headline findings

| Finding | Source | Tier |
|---------|--------|------|
| Resistance training (RT) significantly improves 25/50/100/200m front-crawl times **when combined with aquatic work** | Jin et al. 2024 meta-analysis (Frontiers Physiol) | 1 |
| **Dryland-only** RT improves upper-limb max strength but **does NOT improve swim performance** vs habitual aquatic training | Jin et al. 2024 subgroup analysis | 1 |
| **Aquatic-only** resistance (paddles, parachutes, bands) is **NOT significantly better** than habitual aquatic training | Jin et al. 2024 subgroup analysis | 1 |
| Combined dryland + aquatic ranks highest across multiple performance outcomes (50m time, velocity, stroke rate, stroke length) | 2025 Frontiers network meta-analysis (36 RCTs, 844 swimmers) | 2 |
| RT primarily improves swim performance via increased **stroke rate**, not stroke length | Jin et al. 2024 | 1 |
| **Power training** (low-volume, high-intensity, fast concentric) > max-strength training for swim transfer | Strass 1988 cited in Jin 2024; Crowley 2017 cited in Jin 2024 | 2 |
| 70%+ of propulsion in front crawl comes from upper limbs | Toussaint & Truijens 2005, cited in Aouani 2025 | 2 |

---

## The four training modalities (Jin 2024 framework)

Jin et al. (2024) distinguish four resistance-training types in their RCT meta-analysis:

| Modality | Description | Effect on 25/50/100m | Effect on stroke rate | Effect on stroke length |
|----------|-------------|----------------------|------------------------|---------------------------|
| **CRT** (Concurrent RT) | Dryland RT + aquatic resistance work in the same periodised program | **Significant improvement** | Increased | No change |
| **PT** (Power Training) | Dryland fast-concentric / ballistic / Olympic lifts | **Significant improvement** | (insufficient data) | (insufficient data) |
| **DLRT** (Dry-Land RT alone) | Dryland resistance, no aquatic complement | **NO improvement in swim performance**; improves upper-limb 1RM only | No change | No change |
| **ART** (Aquatic RT alone) | Hand paddles, water parachutes, resistance bands | **NO improvement in any measure** vs habitual aquatic | No change | No change |

**Key insight**: Two things alone don't work — dryland alone, or aquatic resistance alone. The **combination** does. The mechanism appears to be that dryland builds the strength capacity, and the aquatic work teaches the nervous system to deploy that strength in the water-specific motor pattern.

Crowley 2017 (cited by Jin) attributes the dryland-only failure to **fatigue interference** — high-volume dryland might fatigue the swimmer such that the in-water transfer never happens. This is consistent with the next finding:

> "low-volume (≤3 sets, ≤10 repetitions), high-intensity (concentric contractions as fast as possible) resistance training might reduce neuromuscular fatigue and increase strength and neuromuscular improvements" — Crowley 2017, paraphrased in Jin 2024

So the prescription **within** dryland: low volume, high intensity, fast concentric. Not bodybuilding-style hypertrophy work.

---

## Why does it work? (Mechanism)

Two complementary mechanisms identified:

### 1. Neuromuscular adaptations (Strass 1988, cited in Jin 2024)

Strass found that improvements in **maximal power** were more beneficial than improvements in maximal strength. Attributed to:
- Improved motor unit recruitment
- Motor unit synchronization
- Co-contraction patterns
- Rate coding (firing frequency)
- Intra-muscular and inter-muscular coordination
- Reduced neural inhibition

These adaptations transfer to swimming because the **propulsive stroke** demands rapid force production, not just absolute force.

### 2. Stroke-rate increase, not stroke-length (Jin 2024)

The mechanism by which resistance training improves swim time is mostly through **higher stroke rate**, not longer pull per stroke. The exception: very high-intensity, low-rep DLRT (Strass 1988: 3 sets × 3 reps × 90–100% 1RM × 24 sessions / 6 weeks) may improve stroke length specifically.

So:
- Want stroke-rate gains → moderate-load CRT or PT (3–6 sets, 6–10 reps)
- Want stroke-length gains → heavy DLRT (3 × 3 × 90–100% 1RM)
- Want both → unclear; under-studied

### 3. Injury reduction (secondary benefit)

Multiple sources (Tate 2012, Hill 2015, McKenzie 2023, all cited in Jin 2024 introduction) show that strengthening shoulder-stabilizing muscle groups reduces overuse injuries common in swimmers. This is a "free" benefit of consistent dryland regardless of the performance transfer.

## Evidence quality / risk of bias

The Jin 2024 meta-analysis includes a GRADE-style quality rating. Most of their effect estimates carry **"low" or "very low" quality of evidence** ratings due to:
- Small sample sizes (often 20–30 per study)
- Heterogeneity in protocols (intensity, volume, duration vary widely)
- Risk-of-bias issues (allocation concealment, blinding, etc.)

So even though the meta-analysis says "RT works for sprint", **the effect-size confidence intervals are wide**. Translation: the direction of effect is reliable; the magnitude is not.

In specific numeric terms (Jin 2024 effect sizes for short distances):

| Outcome | Pooled effect (95% CrI) | # studies | Quality |
|---------|------------------------|-----------|---------|
| 25m time (s) | −1.03 to −2.35 (varies by RT type) | 4–6 | Very low to Low |
| 50m time (s) | (significant for CRT, PT) | varies | Low |
| 200m time (s) | −4.91 (−8.65, −1.18) overall | 3 | Very low |
| Stroke rate | +0.04 cycles/min | 6 | Very low |

Direction = positive. Magnitude = small to moderate, with low confidence on the exact number.

---

## Where the experimental designs differ in important ways

When reading individual studies cited within these meta-analyses, watch for:

1. **Study population** — most evidence is from **adolescent competitive swimmers** (14–18yo), not masters athletes. Direct transfer to a 31-year-old detrained adult is not validated.
2. **Training experience** — RT effects are larger in resistance-naive participants. A swimmer with 23 years of training history (sprint-specific) may have different ceilings.
3. **Period of intervention** — most are 6–12 weeks. Long-term (1+ year) effects under-studied.
4. **Concurrent training volume** — some studies replace some swim volume with RT; others add RT on top. The "drop swim volume to add RT" question is not resolved.

---

## What the evidence DOESN'T support

Based on the surveyed reviews, weak evidence for:
- **Aquatic-only resistance** (paddles, parachutes, bands) producing measurable sprint improvement — Jin 2024 found no significant effect
- **High-volume bodybuilding-style dryland** (8–15 reps × 4–5 sets) producing better swim transfer than low-volume power work
- **Dryland in isolation** as a substitute for in-water training time

---

## Practical takeaways (research mode — facts only, no prescription)

1. The combination (dryland + aquatic) is the active ingredient; dryland alone is mostly an injury-prevention and strength-building stimulus, not a sprint performance stimulus.
2. Within dryland, low-volume high-intensity / power-style work appears to transfer better than bodybuilding-style hypertrophy work.
3. The strongest single dryland predictor of 50m freestyle is **upper-body Pmax** (covered in `02_sprint-strength-profiles.md`).
4. Effect sizes are real but modest — strength training is not a magic bullet, just one input among several.
