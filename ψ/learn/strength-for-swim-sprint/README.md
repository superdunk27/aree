## TL;DR (3-paragraph version)

**Transfer is real, but conditional**. Resistance training improves 25–200m front-crawl times — provided dryland is **combined** with in-water training (concurrent or alongside). Dryland-only programs increase upper-limb strength but **don't transfer to swim time** without the aquatic component (Jin 2024 meta-analysis subgroup analysis). Aquatic-resistance-only (paddles, parachutes) doesn't help either. The combination is the magic.

**Power, not max strength, is the strongest predictor**. In a 2025 Frontiers study of national-level male sprinters (50m PBs 26.5–28.1s), maximum muscular power assessed via Smith-machine bench-press load-velocity profile correlated **r = −0.86** with 50m time and was the only independent predictor in a regression model (R² = 0.86). Elite swimmers carry 18–25% higher Pmax than sub-elite peers. This converges with the Sprint Factors 2024 systematic review's headline finding: muscular strength in upper and lower limbs is **"velocity-oriented rather than load-oriented"** for swim performance.

**Minimum effective dose is small**. Outside the swim-specific literature, the broader strength-training research has converged on this: as little as **1–3 sets per week per muscle group** at a meaningful intensity is sufficient to drive 1RM gains in beginners, and **6–10 sets/muscle/week** maintains hypertrophy in trained athletes. Single-set protocols and "weekend warrior" patterns are the two best-supported low-dose approaches (Iversen 2024 review). For swim power specifically, the swim literature points to **low-volume, high-intensity** (≤3 sets, ≤10 reps, concentric as fast as possible) as the sweet spot — this minimizes neuromuscular fatigue while maximizing the speed-strength adaptations that transfer to water.

## Open questions Aree noticed during the survey

(For later — not for resolving now.)

1. **Lower-body power's role in 50m freestyle** is under-studied in the literature; most papers focus on upper-body power. Yet starts and turns are lower-body-dominant. The jump-mat project sits exactly on this gap.
2. **Velocity-oriented strength for masters-aged athletes** with detraining backgrounds: the recommendation generalizes from young competitive cohorts; there is some risk that joint-friendly heavy-load work (4–6 reps at 80%+) might be preferable to high-velocity bench throws for someone 31 with 23 years of shoulder mileage. Worth checking shoulder-impingement literature before any heavy ballistic upper-body work.
3. **Sargent jump as predictor of swim sprint** — none of the surveyed papers used it directly. Counter-movement jump (CMJ) shows up occasionally; no clean translation to Toey's measurement methodology yet.
