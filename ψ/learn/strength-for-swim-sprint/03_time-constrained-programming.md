# 03 — Time-Constrained Programming

**Question**: When time is the primary constraint, what's the minimum effective dose for strength gains? What does the research support for "low-frequency, high-quality" approaches?

---

## Headline numbers

| Goal | Minimum effective dose | Source | Tier |
|------|------------------------|--------|------|
| Strength gain (1RM) — beginners | 1 set, 1–3×/week per exercise | Borde et al. cited in Iversen 2024 | 2 |
| Strength gain — powerlifters | 3 working sets/week per lift; or 2-3-1 weekly squat/bench/deadlift | Androulakis-Korakakis et al. 2021 (cited in WebSearch summary) | 2 |
| Hypertrophy maintenance | 6–10 sets/muscle group/week | General consensus, Iversen 2024 review | 2 |
| Power development for swim sprint | ≤3 sets, ≤10 reps, fast concentric, ~3×/week | Crowley 2017 cited in Jin 2024 | 1 (chain to 2) |
| Maximal-strength swim adaptation | 3 sets × 3 reps × 90–100% 1RM, ~4×/week | Strass 1988 cited in Jin 2024 (24 sessions / 6 weeks) | 2 |

The cross-cutting message: **a lot less than people think**. Single sets, 1–3×/week is enough to drive measurable adaptation in untrained-to-moderately-trained populations.

---

## The five minimal-dose strategies (Iversen et al. 2024)

The 2024 Sports Medicine review by Iversen et al. categorized minimal-dose RT strategies:

| Strategy | Description | Evidence support |
|----------|-------------|------------------|
| **"Weekend Warrior"** | Total weekly volume in 1–2 sessions instead of distributed | **Strongly supported** |
| **Single-set RT** | 1 set per exercise, 1–3×/week | **Strongly supported** |
| **RT "Snacking"** | Very short sessions (5–10 min) sprinkled across the week | Emerging, promising |
| **Practicing the strength test** | High-intensity reps that resemble the test | Less direct evidence |
| **Eccentric minimal doses** | Eccentric-only short sessions, often once/week | Emerging, promising |

Source confidence: WebSearch summary (PMC original was blocked). Tier 2.

The two with the strongest evidence: **Weekend Warrior** and **single-set RT**. Both convergent with the broader minimalist-training narrative review (Schoenfeld, Rolnick et al. 2024 — the Springer narrative review):

> "individuals with limited time to exercise or who lack motivation to perform RT are interested in the least amount of RT to improve physical fitness"
> — Schoenfeld et al. 2024

Their conclusion: lower-than-typical-recommended dosages can improve fitness, especially for sedentary individuals or those not currently meeting baseline activity guidelines.

## Within the swim-specific literature

Crowley 2017 (cited in Jin 2024) reached the same conclusion from a different angle: for swim-sprint power transfer, **low volume + high intensity > high volume + moderate intensity**. The mechanism (per Crowley 2017, paraphrased in Jin 2024): low-volume, high-intensity (≤3 sets, ≤10 repetitions, **concentric contractions as fast as possible**) protocols **reduce neuromuscular fatigue** while increasing strength and neuromuscular adaptation.

Specific protocols that worked in published swim studies:

| Study | Protocol | Effect |
|-------|----------|--------|
| Strass 1988 | 3 sets × 3 reps × 90–100% 1RM, 24 sessions / 6 weeks (~4×/wk) | Improved 25m + 50m via stroke length |
| Sammoud 2019, 2021 | 4–6 sets × 6–10 reps, power emphasis | Improved 25/50/100/200m |
| Norberto 2023 | 2–4 sets × 4–10 reps, power emphasis | Improved 25/50/100/200m |
| Girold 2012 | 3 sets × 6 reps × 80–90% 1RM, 12 sessions / 4 weeks (3×/wk) | Improved stroke length + 50m |

Pattern: 2–6 sets, 3–10 reps, 3–4×/week, intensity high (80%+ 1RM or fast concentric). 4–6 weeks is enough to see statistical effects.

---

## Volume is NOT the limiting factor for swim transfer

Amara 2021b (cited in Jin 2024) directly compared three dryland volumes:

- **High**: 5–6 sets × 3–5 reps × 85–95% 1RM
- **Moderate**: 4–5 sets × 3–5 reps × 85–95% 1RM
- **Low**: 3–4 sets × 3–5 reps × 85–95% 1RM

All three groups showed **similar swim performance improvements**. The high-volume group did NOT outperform the low-volume group.

Translation: at high intensity (85–95% 1RM), volume past ~3–4 sets adds nothing for swim transfer. The driver is the intensity / velocity quality, not the total tonnage.

This is a strong argument for the **minimum effective dose is the right dose** philosophy in time-constrained programming. Adding volume on top of a quality stimulus = wasted recovery cost without performance gain.

## Maintenance vs gain

A useful conceptual distinction:

| Phase | Volume needed | Implication |
|-------|---------------|-------------|
| **Initial gain** (untrained → trained) | 4–10 sets/muscle/week | Higher volume drives faster initial gain |
| **Continuing gain** (trained athlete) | 10+ sets/muscle/week (varies a lot) | Plateau-busting requires volume |
| **Maintenance** (already trained, minimal time) | 6–10 sets/muscle/week, even **less for some athletes** | Maintenance is **much cheaper** than gaining |

For a sprint-trained athlete on hiatus, the maintenance phase is the relevant one. The implication: holding 80–90% of strength capacity may take far less work than getting there originally.

The Iversen 2024 review summarizes: "muscle maintenance with as little as 6–10 sets per muscle group per week" is well supported.

## "Snacking" — emerging concept

Some research (per Iversen 2024 summary) supports very short, frequent sessions distributed across the day or week — "RT snacking". E.g., 5 minutes of bodyweight work, 3–4 times a day, 5 days a week.

Strong evidence quality: still emerging.
Practical relevance: useful framing for someone who can't commit a full hour but has 5–10 minute pockets.

---

## Frequency — once/week vs more

The Iversen 2024 review covers Weekend Warrior (1–2 sessions per week clustering all volume). Findings:

- For untrained people, 1×/week is enough to drive 1RM gains (Borde 2015, cited in WebSearch summary)
- For maintenance in already-trained athletes, 1×/week of high-quality work can hold most adaptation
- For continued gain in trained athletes, 2–3×/week is the practical floor; 4+ may add marginal gain

For Toey's profile (23 years sprint-trained, currently 9 months detrained, gym access, full-time job): the relevant range is probably 2–3×/week of quality work, not "snacking" or daily volume. But this is a gain-vs-maintenance judgment Toey makes based on his own goals, not Aree.

## Concurrent training fatigue / interference

A separate but related question: when an athlete trains BOTH endurance (swim) and strength (gym), does the strength session interfere with swim adaptation, or vice versa?

The classic interference effect (Hickson 1980 → many follow-ups) shows that high-volume endurance work blunts strength gains. The reverse — strength blunting endurance — is weaker.

For a sprint swimmer, this is mostly a non-issue:
- Sprint swim training is itself glycolytic/phosphagen-dominant, not aerobic-dominant
- High-intensity, low-volume strength work (the recommended pattern from Section "Within the swim-specific literature" above) carries low fatigue cost
- The two training modes share neuromuscular demands (rapid force production), so adaptations partially overlap

The Garrido et al. 2010 study (cited in Aouani 2025) examined whether combined dryland + aerobic training **inhibits** young competitive swimmers' performance. They found no inhibition — the combination was neutral-to-positive.

But: **detrained adult masters athlete** is a different population than the competitive adolescents the literature covers. The interference question for that profile is under-studied.

## Recovery considerations (brief)

- A 9-month detrained sprint swimmer rebuilding in the gym may have **more than usual** recovery sensitivity for the first 2–4 weeks (delayed-onset muscle soreness from re-introduction of high-intensity work)
- Joint health concern: 23 years of swim shoulder mileage suggests caution with aggressive ballistic upper-body work. Gradual progression matters.
- Sleep, nutrition, life-stress all modulate recovery capacity. Time-constrained programming should match recovery-constrained programming — they're often the same person.

(These are general considerations, not personal prescription. Toey's specific recovery profile is his to assess.)

## What the evidence does NOT support (in time-constrained context)

1. **"You need 5–6 sessions/week to make progress"** — false for beginners and maintenance phase, partly true for trained athletes pushing peak
2. **"Higher volume always wins"** — false, especially for swim transfer (Amara 2021b found no extra benefit beyond ~3–4 sets at high intensity)
3. **"Strength training will make you bulky and slow"** — broadly false; ballistic / power-style work especially does not drive bulk
4. **"You can't make gains in 30 minutes/session"** — false; 30 minutes at high intensity covers most of what's needed for strength + power adaptations

## Synthesis: low-time, swim-sprint-relevant template

(Research-mode synthesis, NOT a prescription for Toey. This is a hypothetical "what would the literature support" template.)

A literature-supported minimum-effective template might look like:

- **2–3 sessions/week**
- **30–45 minutes per session**
- **3–5 compound exercises**: bench-press variant, pull variant, squat or deadlift variant, vertical or horizontal press, optionally Olympic lift derivative or jump variant
- **3 sets × 3–6 reps at 80–90% 1RM** for max-strength exercises
- **3 sets × 3–5 reps with maximal velocity intent** for power exercises (e.g., bench throws, jump squats, ballistic pulls)
- **6-week mesocycle** before re-evaluation

This template is consistent with: Strass 1988, Girold 2012, Crowley 2017 recommendation, Amara 2021b finding (low-volume = high-volume at this intensity), Iversen 2024 minimal-dose review.

It is **not** a personal program for Toey. Coach-mode would require gathering equipment, recovery, injury history, and goal context.
