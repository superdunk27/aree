**Profile:**

- **Era:** 2016–present (post-Rio, peak 2020–2021)

**Philosophy:**
- **Science-Based:** Ph.D. in Exercise Physiology; ASCA Hall of Fame coach

## Part C — Nutrition for Sprinters

### 1. Daily Macronutrient Targets

**Carbohydrates:**
- **Heavy Training Days (1–3 hrs/day):** 6–10 g/kg body weight/day
- **Extreme Training Days (4–5 hrs/day):** 8–12 g/kg body weight/day
- **Rationale:** Glycogen availability supports sprint power output and recovery

**Protein:**
- **ISSN Recommendation:** 1.4–2.0 g/kg body weight/day (standard range)
- **Sprinters Specific:** 1.6–2.2 g/kg supports muscle recovery and power adaptation
- **Distribution:** Evenly spaced protein feedings (approximately every 3 hours during the day)
- **Per Meal:** 20–40 g high-quality protein, 3–4 hours apart

**Fat:**
- **Daily Target:** 20–30% of total caloric intake
- **Minimum:** >20% ensures hormone production and sustained energy availability

**Source:** [ISSN Position Stand: Nutrient Timing](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5596471/) | [ISSN: Protein and Exercise](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12970-017-0177-8) | [USA Swimming Fueling Guide](https://www.usaswimming.org/docs/default-source/camps/national-select-camp/fueling-for-competition.pdf)

### 2. Energy Availability & Red-S Warning

**Critical Concern:** Low Energy Availability (LEA)

The 2023 IOC Consensus Statement on Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport (RED-S) highlights that **under-fueling impairs performance and adaptation**, with consequences for:
- Brain function and mood
- Reproductive health
- Metabolic efficiency
- Immune function
- Bone density

**Sprint-Specific Risk:**
Sprinters are particularly vulnerable because explosive power requires **optimal energy availability**. Even mild LEA (insufficient carbs + protein relative to expenditure) reduces:
- Force production
- Recovery from high-intensity efforts
- Training adaptation

**RED-S Screening:**
Athletes, coaches, and support staff should monitor:
- Unexplained performance plateaus or declines
- Injury frequency
- Menstrual irregularities (female athletes)
- Mood changes, sleep disturbances
- Digestive issues

**Source:** [2023 IOC RED-S Consensus](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37752011/) | [2018 IOC RED-S Update](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29773536/)

### 3. Pre-Race Fueling Strategy (Critical for 50m)

**3–4 Hours Before Race:**
- **Meal:** Full meal with ≥3 food groups
- **Content:** Carbohydrates + moderate protein + minimal fat (easier digestion)
- **Hydration:** Adequate fluid (not excessive)
- **Example:** Oatmeal + banana + egg whites + water

**45–60 Minutes Before Race:**
- **Snack:** Quick-digesting carbohydrates ONLY
- **Avoid:** Protein, fat (digest slowly; won't help acutely)
- **Content:** Fruit (banana, berries), cereal (low fiber), energy gels
- **Hydration:** Sip water (don't over-hydrate)
- **Caffeine Timing:** If using caffeine (see supplements section), consume here for peak effect at race start

**15–30 Minutes Before Race:**
- **Final Top-Off:** Additional fast carbs (box of raisins, energy chews)
- **Final Hydration:** 8–12 oz water
- **Stop Aggressive Hydration:** 30–60 min before first race to allow bladder emptying

**Critical:** Practice this protocol in training. Digestive tolerance is highly individual; race day is not the time to experiment.

**Source:** [USA Swimming Fueling for Competition](https://www.usaswimming.org/docs/default-source/camps/national-select-camp/fueling-for-competition.pdf) | [Train Daly Pre-Race Nutrition](https://www.traindaly.com/train-daly/blog/what-to-eat-before-a-swim-meet-the-best-pre-race-foods)

### 4. Race-Day Hydration

**During Competition:**
- Most 50m races last <25 seconds; minimal hydration needed mid-race
- Focus: Pre-race hydration status (euhydration) before first heat

**Between Heats/Races:**
- Electrolyte drink (sodium + potassium) preferred over water alone
- Typical: Sports drink (4–8% carbohydrate + 10–20 mmol/L sodium)
- Volume: 150–250 mL (5–8 oz) per 15–20 min rest between efforts

**Post-Race (Multi-Race Days):**
- Rehydrate: 1.5 L per kg of body mass loss over next 2–4 hours
- Include sodium (aids retention, stimulates thirst)

**Source:** [USA Swimming Fueling Guide](https://www.usaswimming.org/docs/default-source/camps/national-select-camp/fueling-for-competition.pdf)

### 5. Evidence-Based Supplements for Sprinters

#### **Creatine Monohydrate** — STRONG EVIDENCE

- **Dose:** 3–5 g/day (maintenance after 5–7 day loading at 20 g/day if time-limited)
- **Timing:** Consistent daily ingestion; not acute timing-dependent
- **Evidence:** Well-established for improving power output and repeated sprint capacity
- **Swimmer-Specific:** Demonstrated ergogenic effects in competitive swimmers; faster times in repeat 100m efforts after first heat
- **Safety:** Decades of research; very safe at recommended doses; maintain hydration
- **Mechanism:** Increases phosphocreatine availability for ATP regeneration (critical for 50m explosive efforts)

#### **Beta-Alanine (β-Alanine)** — MODERATE EVIDENCE

- **Dose:** 3–6 g/day (divided into 3–6 × 0.8–1 g doses to avoid paresthesia)
- **Timing:** Daily; acute timing less important than chronic loading
- **Evidence:** May provide slight advantage in events with sustained high-intensity (100–200m swimming, repeated 50m efforts)
- **Swimmer-Specific:** Benefits for 50–200m range events; 50m pure sprints are shorter but back-to-back heats increase fatigue
- **Mechanism:** Buffers lactate/hydrogen ions; improves repeated sprint tolerance
- **Side Note:** High doses cause harmless tingling sensation (paresthesia); lower frequency reduces this

#### **Caffeine** — STRONG EVIDENCE

- **Dose:** 3–6 mg/kg body weight (e.g., 210–420 mg for 70 kg athlete)
- **Timing:** 45–60 minutes pre-race (peak blood levels at race time)
- **Evidence:** Consistent improvements in sprint power, speed, and technical skill execution
- **Swimmer-Specific:** Enhances CNS arousal; particularly useful for early-morning heats
- **Sources:** Coffee (80–100 mg/cup), espresso, caffeine pills, energy drinks
- **Cautions:** Individual sensitivity varies; jitters, anxiety, bathroom urgency possible
- **Timing Note:** Avoid if multiple races <2 hrs apart (accumulation)

#### **Sodium Bicarbonate (Baking Soda)** — MODERATE EVIDENCE (For Repeated Sprints)

- **Dose:** 0.3 g/kg body weight (e.g., 21 g for 70 kg athlete; equivalent to ~1 tbsp baking soda)
- **Timing:** 60–90 minutes pre-race
- **Evidence:** 1.7% performance improvement in high-intensity repeated sprint protocols (relevant for back-to-back heats)
- **Mechanism:** Buffers blood pH; delays fatigue in anaerobic glycolytic efforts
- **Swimmer-Specific:** More effective in races 2–3 (where fatigue accumulates) than race 1
- **Cautions:** GI distress common (nausea, diarrhea); sodium citrate is alternative (takes effect ~3 hrs; may avoid GI issues, but timing different)
- **Practical:** Often mixed with water or sports drink; sip to minimize stomach upset

#### **Sodium Citrate** — ALTERNATIVE

- **Dose:** ~0.5 g/kg body weight (slightly higher than bicarbonate due to lower bioavailability)
- **Timing:** 3 hours pre-race (allows citrate → bicarbonate conversion in blood)
- **Evidence:** Similar pH-buffering effects to sodium bicarbonate, possibly with better GI tolerance
- **Trade-off:** Requires earlier ingestion (less convenient for race logistics)

#### **Supplements NOT Recommended for 50m Sprints:**

- **Beetroot Juice/Nitrates:** Optimized for endurance (>5 min efforts); minimal benefit for 20–25 sec sprints
- **HMB, BCAAs:** Insufficient evidence; protein + whole food more cost-effective

**Source:** [ISSN Practical Issues Evidence-Based Supplements](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5371635/) | [ISSN Sodium Bicarbonate Position Stand](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12970-021-00458-w) | [Sport Supplements Systematic Review](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12498230/)

### 6. Post-Exercise Recovery Nutrition (Golden Window: 30–60 min)

**Immediate Post-Set (0–30 min):**
- **Protein:** 20–25 g high-quality (whey, chicken, fish, dairy)
- **Carbohydrate:** 0.6–1.0 g/kg body mass (e.g., 42–70 g for 70 kg athlete)
- **Ratio Guideline:** 3:1 or 4:1 carbs:protein
- **Purpose:** Maximize muscle protein synthesis (MPS) and glycogen restoration

**Format:** Liquid recovery drink preferred (faster absorption; easier post-exercise)
- Example: Chocolate milk (ideal carb:protein ratio + calcium)
- Alternative: Recovery shake with whey protein + banana + juice

**Carbohydrate Timing:**
- **Hour 1 post-exercise:** 1 g/kg body mass (50–70 g for typical sprinter)
- **Hour 2–6 post-exercise:** 0.6 g/kg every 2 hours
- **Rationale:** Maximizes glycogen repletion for subsequent sessions

**24-Hour Protein Target:**
Total daily protein (1.6–2.2 g/kg) distributed evenly across meals ensures sustained MPS and adaptation.

**Source:** [ISSN Nutrient Timing Position Stand](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5596471/) | [Nutritional Strategies Post-Exercise Recovery](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-025-02213-6) | [USOPC Recovery Nutrition Guide](https://www.usaswimming.org/docs/default-source/camps/national-select-camp/recovery-nutrition.pdf)

### 7. Sleep & Recovery Nutrition (Pre-Sleep Casein Protein)

**Pre-Sleep Protein (30–40 min before bed):**
- **Dose:** 30–40 g casein protein (slow-digesting)
- **Effect:** Acutely increases muscle protein synthesis throughout the night; increases metabolic rate without promoting fat gain
- **Format:** Casein shake, Greek yogurt, cottage cheese

**Timing Logic:**
Sleep is a 7–9 hour fasted period; casein provides steady amino acid availability during this anabolic window.

**Carbohydrate Pre-Sleep:**
- Avoid large pre-sleep meals (digestion disrupts sleep)
- Light carb + protein combo (e.g., banana + casein shake) is acceptable

**Source:** [ISSN Nutrient Timing](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5596471/)

**Performance Correlations (Research-Based):**

*Males:* Protein-Fat Index (balance between muscle and fat) shows strongest correlation with sprint performance (r = 0.392; explains 35.1% of variance). Translation: *muscle quality + leanness matters more than absolute muscularity.*

*Females:* Skeletal muscle mass % shows strongest correlation (r = 0.732; explains 75.1% of variance). Translation: *maintaining muscle mass while minimizing unnecessary fat is critical.*

**Practical Implication:**
- **Don't over-lean:** Excessive body fat reduction (below 8% for males, 13% for females) risks energy deficiency and injury
- **Monitor performance:** Body composition changes should correlate with improved sprint times, not just aesthetics
- **Gender differences:** Female sprinters can afford slightly higher body fat (15–18%) while maintaining elite performance

## Key Takeaways: 5 Actionable Nutrition Principles for 50m Freestylers

### 1. **Protect Energy Availability — Avoid RED-S**
Ensure daily intake meets expenditure. Calculate training load (hours × intensity) and match with sufficient carbs (6–10 g/kg) + protein (1.6–2.2 g/kg). Underfueling kills sprint power. Use energy balance as a fundamental baseline, not body composition targets.

### 2. **Optimize Pre-Race Fueling Window**
- 3–4 hrs before: full balanced meal
- 45–60 min before: fast carbs + caffeine (if tolerated)
- 15–30 min before: final carb top-off
  
*Practice this protocol in training.* Digestive tolerance is individual; race day surprises are costly.

### 3. **Use Creatine Monohydrate (No Debate)**
5g/day, every day. Increases phosphocreatine stores, enabling higher power output in repeated efforts (crucial for heats + finals on same day). Evidence: 25+ years of research; very safe; cost-effective (~$0.05/day).

### 4. **Strategize Caffeine & Sodium Bicarbonate for Race-Specific Context**
- **Single-race day:** Caffeine (3–6 mg/kg, 45–60 min pre-race) for CNS arousal
- **Multi-heat day (back-to-back races):** Sodium bicarbonate (0.3 g/kg, 60–90 min pre-race) buffers fatigue in races 2–3
- **Combination:** Both can be used together (practice in training to assess GI tolerance)

### 5. **Master Post-Exercise Recovery Window (0–60 min)**
- **Within 30 min:** 20–25 g protein + 0.6–1.0 g/kg carbs (3:1 or 4:1 ratio)
- **Example:** Chocolate milk (250 mL) + banana, or whey shake + juice
- **Impact:** Maximizes glycogen repletion + muscle protein synthesis; sets up next session's performance
