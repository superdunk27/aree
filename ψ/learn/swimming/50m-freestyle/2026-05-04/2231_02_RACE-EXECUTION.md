# 50m Freestyle: Race Execution & Phase Analysis
**Date**: 2026-05-04  
**Status**: Long course (50m LCM), no-turn race

---

## 1. Block Start (0–0.6s)

### Track Start vs. Grab Start

Modern competitive swimming uses the **track start** almost exclusively. It provides superior stability on the block, faster reaction times, and better utilization of the starting block wedge for maximum forward power ([Swimming Starts Off Blocks, Dan Swims](https://danswim.com/swimming-starts-off-blocks)).

**Key biomechanics:**
- **Reaction time target**: <0.60 seconds from signal to feet leaving the block
- **Stance width**: Narrow stance produces faster block times and better horizontal force generation than wide stance
- **Weight distribution**: Critical factor for performance. Most elite swimmers prefer a rear or neutral-weighted position, though some perform better front-weighted. A slow reaction time is typically caused by hips positioned too far back ([Swimming Start Reaction Time, Athletes Untapped](https://athletesuntapped.com/blog/the-first-advantage-mastering-start-reaction-time-in-swimming/))
- **Horizontal impulse**: Elite swimmers achieve significantly higher block forces in horizontal and vertical axes than recreational swimmers (3.60 ± 0.23 N/kg vs. 3.17 ± 0.30 N/kg) ([Biomechanical Analysis of the Swim-Start, PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3990873/))

### Entry & Flight Phase

**Entry angle**: Elite swimmers maintain low take-off angles of approximately 21.25 ± 5.59° for optimal distance and water entry quality ([Biomechanical Analysis of the Swim-Start, PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3990873/)).

**Common faults**:
- Extended time between reaction signal and block impulse
- Poor transition timing between gliding and leg kicking phases
- Early arm stroking (generates more drag than continued gliding)
- Inadequate body streamlining during underwater phases
- Placing hands in shoulder alignment instead of one on top of the other (causes 7% increase in resistance) ([Biomechanical Analysis of the Swim-Start, PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3990873/))

## 2. Underwater Dolphin Kick (0.6–3.5s / 0–15m)

### FINA 15-Meter Rule

World Aquatics regulations limit submerged distance after start and each turn to 15 metres maximum. This rule preserves the stroke component of swimming while allowing explosive underwater phases ([Why is the dolphin kick limited to 15 meters, Quora](https://www.quora.com/Why-is-the-dolphin-kick-limited-to-15-meters-for-the-freestyle-swimming-races)).

### Kick Frequency, Amplitude & Speed

**Elite performance targets:**
- **Kick count**: 5–7 dolphin kicks for the 50m start (varies by individual)
- **Kick speed**: >2.8 m/s at the fastest segments
- **Distance covered**: Elite sprinters like Caeleb Dressel travel 15m in under 5 seconds
- **Key principle**: Kick speed is the differentiator, not kick distance. Many elite swimmers approach the 15m limit, but what separates faster swimmers is how quickly they move underwater ([How the Underwater Dolphin Kick Evolved, Swimming World Magazine](https://www.swimmingworldmagazine.com/news/how-the-underwater-dolphin-kick-evolved-and-revolutionized-the-sport/))

### Individual Variation

Gretchen Walsh and Caeleb Dressel represent opposite strategies:
- **Walsh**: Longest possible underwater distance, exceptional dolphin kick speed and technique
- **Dressel**: Shorter underwater distance (6 down kicks) with explosive surface acceleration

Both work because they optimize their personal kick-to-surface swimming speed ratio ([Training Strategies for Faster Underwaters, SwimSwam](https://swimswam.com/training-faster-underwater-dolphin-kick/)).

### Depth Tradeoff

- **Deeper position**: Reduces form drag and drag forces underwater (advantageous at high speed)
- **Shallower position**: Easier transition to surface swimming, faster breakout entry
- No universal optimal depth—depends on individual streamline efficiency and surface swimming speed ([How Far Underwater Should You Be Dolphin Kicking, Your Swim Log](https://www.yourswimlog.com/how-far-underwater-should-you-be-kicking/))

## 3. Breakout & First Stroke (3.5–8s / 15–25m)

### Timing & Head Position

The breakout marks the transition from underwater propulsion to surface swimming. Timing the final dolphin kick as the head breaks surface optimizes momentum transfer.

**Elite techniques:**
- **Dolphin kick breakout method**: Final dolphin kick coincides with initiation of the first freestyle stroke, creating a single explosive impulse rather than two separate actions
- **Head position at breakout**: Caeleb Dressel tilts his head forward slightly (faster entry into stroke), while Bruno Fratus and Florent Manaudou hold their heads in neutral alignment ([5 Subtle Techniques of Caeleb Dressel 50m Freestyle, The Race Club](https://theraceclub.com/aqua_note/caeleb-dressel-olympic-victory-technique/))
- **Streamlining**: Minimal head movement during breakout; maintain horizontal body position

### Pace Setting

The 15–25m segment (10 seconds) establishes the race's trajectory. Elite swimmers treat this phase as acceleration toward peak velocity, not yet maximum effort.

**Strategic consideration**: Some swimmers build gradually; others explode immediately. Dressel's approach: immediate dominance (0.4s ahead after 25m at Tokyo 2020 Olympics vs. Manaudou and Fratus) ([Tokyo 2020 Olympic Games Men's 50M Freestyle Analysis, Analysis Swim](https://www.analysisswim.com/swim/article/tokyo-2020-2021-summer-olympics-games-race-analysis-man-50m-freestyle-lcm)).

## 4. Surface Swimming Phase (8–45s / 25–45m)

### Stroke Rate & Stroke Length

**Elite profile:**
- **Stroke rate**: 60–62 strokes per minute (SPM), with full cycle lasting ~1.0 second
- **Stroke length**: 2.00–2.20 meters per stroke (elite males)
- **Correlation with speed**: In 50m sprints, stroke length shows strongest positive correlation with velocity (ρ = 0.57 for men), while stroke rate correlations are trivial ([Stroke Rate–Stroke Length Dynamics in Elite Freestyle Swimming, Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/sports-and-active-living/articles/10.3389/fspor.2025.1656633/full))

**Key principle**: Preserving stroke length is essential; increased frequency alone does not guarantee faster times. Efficiency requires "continuous propulsion with no dead spots" ([50m Freestyle Technique: Sprint Champions, Sweep Swimming](https://www.sweepswimming.com/post/50m-freestyle-technique-sprint-swimming-form)).

### Stroke Mechanics

- **Catch phase**: High elbow catch for powerful pull
- **Pull & push**: Full extension and drive
- **Recovery**: Straight-arm recovery to maximize speed and turnover
- **Body alignment**: Horizontal and rigid, with minimal head movement

### Breathing Strategy

**The no-breath norm**: Most elite 50m freestylers (Dressel, Proud, McEvoy) hold their breath the entire race—zero to one breaths over 20–21 seconds.

**Why elite sprinters avoid breathing:**
1. **Stroke disruption**: A breath introduces asymmetry and shortens the stroke on one side; neuromuscular efficiency drops sharply in high-rate coordination events
2. **Aerobic contribution**: Only ~4% of energy comes from aerobic metabolism; ATP-PC system dominates ([Why Elite Sprinters Don't Breathe in 50 Freestyle, Your Swim Log](https://www.yourswimlog.com/sprint-freestyle-breathing/))
3. **Body position advantage**: Full lungs increase buoyancy, keeping the upper body riding higher on the surface and reducing drag ([Why 50 Freestylers Hold Their Breath, Brett Hawke](https://www.bretthawke.com/blog/why-50-freestylers-hold-their-breath-and-should-you))
4. **Rhythm maintenance**: Breath-holding preserves perfect body alignment and timing consistency

**Debate**: Some mid-level swimmers attempt one strategic breath at 25m or 35m for safety/comfort. Elite swimmers' evidence strongly favors no breath for optimal time ([How to Breathe Less in 50m Freestyle, The Swimming Expert](https://theswimmingexpert.com/how-to-breathe-less-in-a-50m-freestyle-race/)).

## 5. Finish Phase (45–50s / 45–50m)

### Touch Technique & Wall Contact

**Critical principle**: The last stroke must hit the wall at full extension. Gliding or shortened strokes at finish cost measurable time.

**Execution:**
- 8–12 meters from finish: Focus entirely on the finish without breathing
- Extend arm fully into wall at the moment of maximum reach
- Avoid gliding after full extension (loses speed between extension and contact)
- Don't lift head to look at wall; keep hips high to minimize excess drag ([50m Freestyle Technique: Sprint Champions, Sweep Swimming](https://www.sweepswimming.com/post/50m-freestyle-technique-sprint-swimming-form))

### Stroke Length Adjustment

As fatigue accumulates (44–50m), tempo naturally slows. Elite sprinters compensate by maintaining stroke length through these final strokes rather than increasing rate. The final 3–4 strokes must be as powerful as the first, preserving range of motion despite central nervous system fatigue ([50m Freestyle Technique: Sprint Champions, Sweep Swimming](https://www.sweepswimming.com/post/50m-freestyle-technique-sprint-swimming-form)).

**Common time losses**: Shortening the final stroke, reaching less fully, or touching with bent arm can cost 0.05–0.15 seconds in a sprint this short.

## 6. Race Timing & Splits

### How Splits Are Measured

International competition timing uses electronic touchpads triggered by hand contact:
- **15m split**: (0–15m underwater phase + early surface)
- **25m split**: (full first 25m)
- **35m split**: (25–35m midrace segment)
- **50m split**: (finish)

### Elite Split Profiles

| Profile | 15m (s) | 25m (s) | 35m (s) | 50m (s) | Athlete Examples |
|---------|---------|---------|---------|---------|-----------------|
| **Sub-21** | ~4.8–5.0 | ~10.8–11.0 | ~16.5–16.8 | <21.0 | Caeleb Dressel (20.16 SCM), Cesar Cielo (20.51 LCM) |
| **21–22s** | ~5.1–5.3 | ~11.1–11.4 | ~16.9–17.3 | 21.0–22.0 | Florent Manaudou (21.19 LCM), Olympic medalists |
| **22–25s** | ~5.5–5.8 | ~11.7–12.2 | ~17.8–18.5 | 22.0–25.0 | Advanced regional swimmers |
| **25–28s** | ~6.0–6.5 | ~12.5–13.2 | ~19.0–20.0 | 25.0–28.0 | Intermediate/collegiate swimmers |

**Key pattern**: Elite swimmers front-load the first 15m (underwater + early surface) because that's where the highest velocity is attained. The 15–25m segment shows less improvement than the first 15m. The final 15m (35–50m) shows the largest slowdown due to fatigue ([50m Freestyle in 21, 22, 23 seconds, Bournemouth Eprints](https://eprints.bournemouth.ac.uk/36483/1/Augusto_Main%20document_IJPAIS.pdf)).

### Tokyo 2020 Olympic Final Example

**Caeleb Dressel (Gold, 21.07)**:
- 15m: ~4.9s
- 25m: ~10.9s
- Led Manaudou by ~0.4s at halfway point

**Florent Manaudou (Silver, 21.55)**:
- 15m: ~5.1s
- 25m: ~11.3s
- 6 down kicks to breakout

**Bruno Fratus (Third)**:
- Approximately 0.5s behind Dressel at 25m mark

Dressel's dominance came from a superior start and underwater phase, not a closing kick—elite 50m races are decided in the first half ([Tokyo 2020 Olympic Games Men's 50M Freestyle, Analysis Swim](https://www.analysisswim.com/swim/article/tokyo-2020-2021-summer-olympics-games-race-analysis-man-50m-freestyle-lcm)).

## 7. Long Course (LCM) vs. Short Course (SCM): Underwater Contribution

### Fundamental Difference: Turns

- **Long course (50m)**: One race, no turn. No underwater phase after start (only the 15m start + breakout).
- **Short course (25m)**: Two 25m lengths with one turn. Extra 15m underwater at the turn point.

### Underwater Time Budget

- **Short course (50m SCM)**: Up to 60% of total race time can be underwater (start + turn + breakout)
- **Long course (50m LCM)**: Up to 30% of race time is underwater (start phase + breakout only)

([How to Race Short Course vs. Long Course, MySwimPro](https://blog.myswimpro.com/2024/06/03/how-to-race-short-course-vs-long-course-in-swimming/))

### Performance Implications

**Why short course times are faster:**
1. Pushing off the wall is faster than any stroke—velocity immediately after turn exceeds surface speed
2. Extended underwater distance allows muscle recovery (lactate clearance, H+ ion reduction)
3. Higher total energy output throughout the race due to rest periods

**Elite time gap**: Top swimmers consistently set faster SCM times than LCM times. Caeleb Dressel holds:
- **Short course**: 20.16 (50m SCM)
- **Long course**: 21.04 (50m LCM)
- **Difference**: ~0.88 seconds (~4% slower in LCM)

([Long Course vs. Short Course, Swimming World Magazine](https://www.swimmingworldmagazine.com/news/long-course-vs-short-course-a-look-at-some-differences/))

### LCM-Specific Strategy

The absence of a turn in 50m LCM means:
- **No mid-race recovery**: Entire race is flat-out effort
- **Back-half execution critical**: Unlike 100m where the turn provides psychological reset, 50m LCM demands consistent pace
- **Finish psychology**: No reprieve—the wall approaches continuously with no turn to reset focus

## Key Debates in 50m Execution

### 1. Breakout Distance: Push the Full 15m or Exit Early?

**Advocates for full 15m**: Specialists like Gretchen Walsh use maximum underwater distance paired with exceptional kick speed.

**Advocates for shorter underwaters**: Dressel and other sprinters exit around 12–13m to maximize surface swimming time at peak velocity.

**Evidence**: Kick speed matters more than distance. The optimal strategy is individual—depends on personal kicking speed vs. surface speed ratio ([How many dolphin kicks, The Race Club](https://theraceclub.com/aqua_note/dolphin-kick-starts-and-turns/)).

### 2. Start Strategy: Explosive First Impulse vs. Controlled Weight Shift

**Explosive approach**: Maximum horizontal force immediately (Dressel's model).

**Controlled approach**: Lower reaction time via front-weighted stance, then accelerate.

**Evidence**: Fastest sprinters show varied approaches. Reaction time <0.60s is non-negotiable; the trade-off between reaction speed and impulse magnitude is individual ([Improve Your Track Start, The Race Club](https://theraceclub.com/aqua_note/improve-your-track-start-in-swimming/)).

### 3. First-Half vs. Second-Half Dominance

In 50m LCM, the race is decided by 25m. Back-half acceleration is minimal because peak velocity is already achieved. Strategy focuses on:
- Explosive start/underwater (0–15m)
- Maintain peak surface velocity (15–50m)
- Minimal deceleration in final 15m

There is no "negative split" in 50m; front-loading is essential ([50m Freestyle in 21, 22, 23 seconds, Bournemouth Eprints](https://eprints.bournemouth.ac.uk/36483/1/Augusto_Main%20document_IJPAIS.pdf)).

## 3–5 Actionable Takeaways for Race Execution

### 1. **Master the Block Start First**

The start contributes up to 30% of total race time. Reaction time <0.60s is the baseline; beyond that, focus on horizontal force generation with a narrow stance and optimal center-of-mass positioning. Even 0.05s of improvement in block time translates to ~0.25s over the full race.

**Action**: Film your block phase, measure reaction time and block velocity. Experiment with weight distribution (front vs. rear) to find your optimal balance between speed and force.

### 2. **Tailor Underwater Distance to Your Kick-to-Sprint Speed Ratio**

Not all swimmers optimize at 15m underwater. Test breakout distances from 12–15m and measure splits at 15m. Whichever distance yields the fastest 15m time is your optimal point. Most elite sprinters find their sweet spot at 13–15m.

**Action**: In training, practice 50m sprints with timed breakouts at 12m, 13m, 14m, and 15m. Record which yields fastest 25m time and lowest velocity drop-off.

### 3. **Eliminate Breathing; Build Oxygen Tolerance Carefully**

No-breath racing is non-negotiable for sub-22 times. Breath-holding disrupts the neuromuscular timing essential for 60–62 SPM and stroke length preservation. However, the switch from breathing to non-breathing requires gradual adaptation to avoid panic.

**Action**: Build hypoxic tolerance progressively. Weeks 1–2: practice holds at 50m with 1 breath. Weeks 3–4: no breath at 50m in practice. Only race non-breathing once fully adapted.

### 4. **Prioritize Stroke Length over Stroke Rate**

The 60–62 SPM sprinters maintain stroke lengths of 2.0–2.20m—this is tight but long. Do not increase rate to >65 SPM at the expense of distance per stroke. Velocity is proportional to (stroke length × stroke rate); maximize both, but length is the primary lever in 50m.

**Action**: Count strokes per 25m in sprint efforts. Elite males take 22–24 strokes per 25m. If you exceed 26, you're prioritizing frequency over distance—refocus.

### 5. **Finish at Full Extension; Never Glide into the Wall**

The last stroke is often where time is lost. Swimmers glide or shorten their final reach by 5–10cm due to fatigue and wall anxiety. This costs 0.05–0.15 seconds in a 50m race.

**Action**: In every race-pace 50m rep, cue "full reach on the last stroke" 8m from the wall. Film the finish phase to verify arm fully extends at touch, not before. Practice finish sequences under high fatigue (post-kicking sets) to ingrain the reflex.
