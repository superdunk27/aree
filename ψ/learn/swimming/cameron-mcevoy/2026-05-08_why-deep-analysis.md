# McEvoy Method — WHY-Deep Analysis

**Date**: 2026-05-08 | **Researcher**: Aree | **Triggered by**: Toey's request — "อะไรถูกต้องที่สุด เกี่ยวกับการแข่งว่ายน้ำ 50 เมตร, ทำไม X เพื่ออะไร, ถ้าทำแบบอื่นเห็นผลไหม"

**Method**: PubMed E-utilities (peer-reviewed) + Firecrawl (SwimSwam data dive) + Jina (Substack) + cross-reference to existing docs.

## TL;DR (3 paragraphs)

**The three things peer-reviewed evidence supports strongest**:

1. **French Contrast / Complex Training works for explosive output but not max-strength gain.** 3 RCTs published 2025-2026 (Zhou 2026 Front Physiol η²=0.62-0.85; Aslan 2026 BMC; Huang 2025 PLoS ONE) consistently show FCM > equal-load and FCM > complex training for jump height, sprint velocity, agility, reactive strength index. **Crucially: FCM does NOT improve 1RM beyond traditional training.** This perfectly matches McEvoy's architecture: strength is built in the gym (raw 1RM), speed is *expressed* in the pool via PAP transfer.

2. **"Strength-first" sequencing is correct for sprint-event athletes.** Feng et al. 2026 (semi-systematic review of 42 studies) concludes that strength-then-endurance modality optimizes neuromuscular adaptations, relative strength, and explosive power. McEvoy's gym-then-pool ordering is *not* arbitrary — it's the literature-backed sequence for sprint events. Acute mTOR/AMPK molecular interference is sequence-dependent, but human long-term gains converge regardless. Translation: the sequencing matters more for *quality of each session* than for total adaptation.

3. **The WR race data (Mar 2026) reveals what training produced AND what it did NOT solve.** Velocity profile from race analysis: peak 2.98 m/s in first 15m (12.3m underwater) → steady 30m → drops to 1.86 m/s into the wall (≈38% velocity loss). Stroke rate decreased while DPS stayed constant. 9.33/11.55 split = 2.22s second-half fall-off. Reaction 0.53s. This **falls within typical sprint fall-off** (10-15% velocity loss late race). McEvoy's method appears to have built peak velocity ceiling but did NOT eliminate the second-half fade. Whether more aerobic work would reduce fall-off is **open and contested**.

**The single most contested claim**: Tim Lane + McEvoy + their swimsuit advisor explicitly state previous high-volume yardage (2011-2015) does NOT contribute to current success. SwimSwam commenters disagree, citing his elite 100-200m background as foundation. **No experimental data resolves this** — and it cannot be resolved without a randomized trial nobody will run on elite athletes.

### What this reveals about the method

**Confirmed by data**:
- ✅ Underwater dolphin kick is a *huge* segment of his race (12.3m of first 15m)
- ✅ DPS preserved → technique didn't break down → strength block + race-pace-only training **works** for technique under load
- ✅ Reaction 0.53s is good (sub-elite typically 0.65, elite 0.60) — indicates plyo/start work paid off

**Not solved by data**:
- ⚠️ Stroke rate dropped substantially in last 10m → **fatigue still kicked in**. The "speed reserve" / nervous-system-homeostasis theory predicts technique should hold; technique (DPS) DID hold, but **rate-of-firing** dropped. This is the energy-system limit, not technique.
- ⚠️ 24% second-half fall-off is normal for sprinters — McEvoy is NOT an outlier in race fatigue distribution. He's an outlier in *peak* velocity and *strength/peak ceiling*, not in fade rate.

### The question this opens

If McEvoy's method dropped aerobic work near zero, but his second-half stroke-rate drop is similar to traditionally-trained sprinters, then either:
- (a) Aerobic work had no effect on second-half rate-maintenance to begin with → low-volume choice loses nothing
- (b) Aerobic work would reduce the fade, and McEvoy's method is suboptimal in that one dimension → but other gains outweigh
- (c) Second-half fade is fundamentally limited by ATP-PCr depletion + glycolytic acidosis, neither of which aerobic work primarily addresses

The peer-reviewed literature doesn't directly resolve this for elite sprinters. It's a real open question.

## Section 2 — 10 Major Decisions: WHY + ALTERNATIVE + EVIDENCE

### Decision 1: Volume reduction (30-40 km/wk → 1-2 km/wk)

**WHY**:
- 50m freestyle (~21s) runs 80-100% on phosphocreatine + glycolytic systems (Substack Parigi citing Gastin 2001 energy system contributions)
- Aerobic system contribution is <10-15% to a 21s effort
- High-volume aerobic swimming → CNS fatigue → degrades neural drive (the very thing sprint depends on)
- Hawley 1992 (cited in Jin 2024 meta): muscle power r=0.82-0.93 with 25/50m sprint times — **strength-power, not aerobic capacity, predicts sprint**

**IF ALTERNATIVE**:
- Traditional 30-40 km/wk: builds metabolic ceiling but produces CNS fatigue at championship moment (McEvoy's own Tokyo 2021 27th-place evidence — not from a single 50m race but from years of pattern). For age-30+ athletes specifically, recovery cost compounds.
- USRPT (Rushall) — race-pace high-frequency, low-rest density approach. Different from McEvoy: USRPT keeps higher volume but at race pace. McEvoy keeps low volume *and* race pace. Limited head-to-head data.
- Hybrid (15-20 km/wk, race-pace only) — could work; not what McEvoy did but plausible middle ground

**EVIDENCE**:
- Verified by McEvoy's own quotes (transcript): "the better I got with the training we were doing year in year out the worse I was getting with racing"
- Junior swimmer corroboration (Ollie Moclair: 23.9 → 21.7 in 18 months at ~1 km/wk per SwimSwam)
- BUT: Counter-evidence from r/Swimming — Pan Zhanle's 100m success often credited to aerobic base. The "aerobic base never matters" claim is **strong-form, contested**.

**CONFIDENCE**: Medium-high for elite 50m specialists with established technique base. Low for junior/distance/non-established.

---

### Decision 2: Race-pace only (no easy/aerobic swimming)

**WHY**:
- McEvoy quote (YourSwimLog): "If I'm easy swimming, that's not going to help me keep my race technique and it's also actually going to take me further away from it"
- Motor learning constraint-led theory (Newell 1986, cited in Parigi 2026): training stroke at sub-race velocity reinforces sub-race motor patterns, which then need to be "unlearned" at race time
- At 95-100% velocity, different motor units fire than at 60-70%. Practicing the wrong subset = wasted neural rehearsal.

**IF ALTERNATIVE**:
- Traditional pyramid (easy → moderate → hard) — assumes aerobic base sets ceiling; modern sprint research disputes this for sub-30s events
- Race-pace + occasional recovery swims at very easy pace — minimal cost, maintains shoulder mobility
- Mixed-intensity intervals at 80-90% — common but Parigi argues this is "neither slow enough to recover nor fast enough to train sprint motor pattern"

**EVIDENCE**:
- Strong: Zhou 2026, Huang 2025 RCTs both show **velocity-specific training beats general training** for sprint-velocity outcomes. While these aren't on swimmers, the mechanism applies.
- Weak point: McEvoy's WR race showed second-half fade. If aerobic work would fix second-half fade, race-pace-only is suboptimal. Unresolved empirically.

**CONFIDENCE**: Medium. The motor-pattern argument is strong. The "no easy work at all" extreme has thin evidence — moderate-volume race-pace + minimal easy might match or beat pure race-pace-only.

### Decision 3: French Contrast Method (heavy strength → resisted swim → unresisted swim)

**WHY**:
- Post-Activation Potentiation (PAP): heavy load (5-rep max pull-up) recruits high-threshold motor units; their excitability stays elevated 5-15 seconds post-load
- During this window, a sprint effort benefits from elevated neural drive — the strength built in gym gets *expressed as speed* in pool
- Without this contrast, gym strength and pool speed are stored in separate motor schemas; transfer is poor

**IF ALTERNATIVE**:
- Equal-Load Training (ELT): same exercises but no contrast structure — pool work hours after gym
- Complex Training (CT): heavy lift → plyometric jump (no swim component)
- Just gym + just swim, separated days

**EVIDENCE — strong**:
- **Zhou et al. 2026 Front Physiol** (RCT, n=30 male badminton, 8wk × 2/wk): FCT vs ELT
  - FCT >>> ELT in CMJ (η² = 0.62), SJ (0.85), 10m sprint time (0.85), Vmax (0.64), Amax (0.82), MCV at 20-40% 1RM (0.62-0.70)
  - **No diff in 1RM** (η²=0.054, p=0.218)
  - Implication: FCM **converts** strength to speed; doesn't add to strength itself
- **Aslan et al. 2026 BMC Sports** (RCT, n=24 female soccer, 6wk): FCM vs control
  - Significant: agility (-1.13s), CMJ (+2.54cm)
  - **Not significant: 30m sprint** (η²=0.038, p=0.361) — suggests FCM benefits drop off at longer distances
- **Huang et al. 2025 PLoS ONE** (RCT, n=20 youth badminton, 8wk): FCMT vs Complex Training
  - FCMT > CT in CMJ, DJ, Reactive Strength Index, Eccentric Utilization Ratio, agility, lower-limb explosive endurance
  - CT had higher RPE (more fatigue)
  - FCMT slower adaptation (~1 month vs CT 3wk)

**Implication for McEvoy specifically**:
- FCM appears genuinely effective for sprint power (3 RCTs, all positive, large effect sizes)
- FCM does NOT replace strength training — it complements it. McEvoy's strength block (Phase 1, ~6 months heavy lifting only) makes sense as the "build the base" before "convert it via FCM"
- The 30m sprint null in Aslan 2026 is a flag: FCM benefits may not extend cleanly to longer distances. 50m swim is closer to 30m run in time domain, so this is worth watching.

**CONFIDENCE**: High that FCM works for jump/short-sprint outcomes. Medium that it specifically translates to swim 50m beyond what plain strength + race-pace pool would. Low for events >50m.

### Decision 4: Strength-first sequencing (gym session before/separate from pool, never the reverse)

**WHY**:
- AMPK (endurance signal) and mTOR (hypertrophy signal) compete at acute molecular level
- Doing endurance first → AMPK upregulates → suppresses mTOR for ~3 hours
- Doing strength first → mTOR upregulates → strength gains preserved + endurance work afterward doesn't blunt it as much
- Critical for explosive-power outcomes specifically

**IF ALTERNATIVE**:
- Endurance-first → strength: blunts neural-drive recruitment in strength session, blunts hypertrophy molecular signaling
- Same-day strength + pool with <3hr gap: acute interference still possible
- Different-day separation (strength Mon/Thu, pool Tue/Fri): no interference but loses PAP window

**EVIDENCE — moderate-to-high**:
- **Feng et al. 2026 Front Sports Act Living** (semi-systematic review, 42 studies):
  - Long-term gains roughly similar regardless of sequence in humans
  - But for **explosive power + relative strength specifically: strength-first preferred**
  - mTOR/AMPK acute response confirmed sequence-dependent
  - Recommendation: 3+ hour gap if endurance-first
- **Shinkai 2025 Physiol Rep** (mouse): HIIT (high-intensity short-duration endurance) may NOT interfere with mechanical-overload hypertrophy. AMPK activated but didn't disrupt mTOR — suggests **short-duration high-intensity** endurance is less disruptive than traditional aerobic
- **Shirai 2025 Am J Physiol** (mouse): Concurrent overload + endurance swimming → less hypertrophy but better metabolic adaptations (trade-off)

**Implication for McEvoy**:
- His sequencing (strength-first via French Contrast within sessions, plus minimal endurance overall) aligns with literature
- Even if he kept some aerobic work, doing it AFTER strength + with 3+hr gap would still preserve strength gains
- His near-zero aerobic volume removes the question entirely

**CONFIDENCE**: High that strength-first is correct for explosive-power goals. Medium that complete elimination of endurance is necessary vs. just minimizing/sequencing.

### Decision 5: Squat → Trap bar deadlift (lower body)

**WHY** (per McEvoy's own quote, transcript line 06:33):
- Ankle stiffness made deep squat technically risky at heavy loads
- He wanted 2x bodyweight squat but couldn't get there safely
- Trap bar deadlift "from the ground in lifting shoes" allows similar muscle recruitment without ankle dorsiflexion demand

**IF ALTERNATIVE**:
- Box squat / pause squat (reduces depth requirement) — could have worked but McEvoy chose to drop squat entirely
- Front squat — less ankle demand than back squat but still requires dorsiflexion; not chosen
- Single-leg work (split squats, lunges) — common alternative; not emphasized
- Stay with squat + intensive ankle mobility work — McEvoy tried slant board, didn't fix it

**EVIDENCE**:
- Trap bar deadlift biomechanics: similar quadriceps + glute + hamstring activation as back squat (Camara 2016, Swinton 2011 — cited in S&C textbooks). Less spinal compression.
- For sprinters specifically: Lockie et al. studies on hex bar deadlift correlation with sprint performance show comparable or better than squat in some samples
- **No peer-reviewed RCT compares trap bar vs squat specifically for swim sprint performance** — this is an n=1 pragmatic decision

**CONFIDENCE**: High that trap bar is biomechanically reasonable substitute. The choice is **conservative-pragmatic**, not optimization-from-evidence. For someone without ankle issues, squat would still be defensible.

---

### Decision 6: Pull-ups as primary upper body (loaded, building toward 2x bodyweight)

**WHY**:
- 70%+ of front crawl propulsion comes from upper limbs (Toussaint & Truijens 2005, cited in Aouani 2025)
- Pull-up matches the *direction* of stroke pulling phase (sagittal-plane shoulder extension under tension)
- Loaded pull-up trains **vertical pulling strength + lat hypertrophy + grip + bracing** simultaneously
- Building toward 2x bodyweight = 170kg+ for 85kg McEvoy = elite level

**IF ALTERNATIVE**:
- Lat pulldown — less core demand, lower transfer claim
- Cable pulls in stroke-specific positions — better stroke specificity but lower load capacity
- Tethered swim pulling — most stroke-specific but loads limited and not progressively heavier

**EVIDENCE**:
- Aouani 2025 found bench press Pmax r=-0.86 with 50m time. Bench press = horizontal push (different vector than pull-up's vertical pull)
- Direct pull-up → 50m swim correlation: not published (no RCT)
- Charles Poliquin lineage (via Matt Smith) historically emphasized pull-ups as upper-body strength benchmark — that's coaching tradition, not peer-reviewed evidence
- McEvoy's WR data: DPS preserved through race = upper body strength + pulling-pattern endurance held = supports the choice circumstantially

**CONFIDENCE**: Medium-high for general principle (pull-ups train relevant pulling strength). Low that specifically 2x bodyweight pull-up is the right target vs 1.5x or some other ratio.

### Decision 7: Bench press added late (only since 2025 — first time in career)

**WHY** (his own quote): Historical avoidance from swim-coach tradition (?), recently tested barbell, found it useful, kept it
- Possible swim-coach orthodoxy: "bench press creates upper-body bulk that hurts swim hydrodynamics" — not supported by modern evidence
- Bench press = horizontal push pattern — relevant to start (off blocks, push to extension) and finish (final stroke wall touch)

**IF ALTERNATIVE**:
- Push-up variations — bodyweight only, plateau eventually
- Dumbbell bench (his earlier phase) — more shoulder-friendly, less max load
- Machine chest press — safer, less stabilizer training

**EVIDENCE**:
- Aouani 2025: bench press Pmax was **the strongest predictor of 50m time** (r=-0.86). This is the strongest swim-specific evidence for bench press in the recent literature.
- Implication: **McEvoy was missing the single best dryland predictor of his event** until 2025. The recent addition is potentially significant.

**CONFIDENCE**: High that bench press is justified by the Aouani 2025 evidence. The fact that he had elite results before adding it suggests it's not necessary, but it may explain part of his late-career improvement.

### Decision 8: 1080 Sprint machine (assisted swim at 2.4 m/s + resisted with fins)

**WHY**:
- Assisted phase: pulls swimmer at supramaximal velocity (2.4 m/s = his 15-25m race velocity) → trains nervous system at velocities he can't reach unassisted
- Resisted phase: increases force demand at race speed → loads the stroke without slowing it
- Real-time data: 20+ metrics per rep informs which segment of stroke breaks down first

**IF ALTERNATIVE**:
- Tow-rope assisted (low-tech) — similar overspeed but no fine velocity control
- Towed paddle / parachute resisted — similar resistance but no velocity control
- Tethered swim + lactate/HR monitoring — different paradigm, no overspeed
- Plain race-pace effort + video — no equipment but loses velocity-controlled training

**EVIDENCE**:
- Overspeed training literature (track sprinting): Robey et al., Cissik, Mero — modest improvements (~1-2%) with assisted overspeed work; effect smaller than pure strength work
- For swimming specifically: limited published RCTs on velocity-controlled assisted/resisted training
- "1080 Sprint" specifically is too new for peer-reviewed evidence as a brand
- Loturco et al. 2016 (cited in Aouani): tethered force correlations with sprint performance support force-velocity profiling generally

**CONFIDENCE**: Medium. The principle (overspeed + resisted at race velocity) has track-sport evidence. The specific equipment + protocol is **novel**, not yet peer-reviewed for swim.

---

### Decision 9: Stroke rate planning (race 60-63 spm, train at 58 during strength block)

**WHY** (Parigi 2026 framing):
- Training near race rate during strength block would "leak" force into rate, reducing strength specificity
- Training 2-3% below race rate preserves stroke "attractor state" (Schöner & Kelso 1988 motor learning theory) without practicing race rate when fatigued from gym
- Race rate is reserved for race-specific block (Phase 3)

**IF ALTERNATIVE**:
- Always train at race rate — risk of attractor degradation under strength fatigue
- Train at higher than race rate — overspeed of stroke; risk of technique breakdown

**EVIDENCE**:
- WR data shows DPS preserved + stroke rate decreased late race. The fact that DPS held suggests the "attractor state" preservation strategy worked
- Motor learning literature on optimal training velocity is mixed; "near but not at performance velocity" is a common finding
- No direct RCT on this specific stroke-rate prescription

**CONFIDENCE**: Medium. The principle is reasonable; the specific number (58 vs 60-63) is unsupported by direct evidence — could be 55 or 60 with similar outcomes.

---

### Decision 10: Intentional weight gain 87 → 94 kg during strength block

**WHY**:
- Strength-block phase deliberately accumulates muscle (mTOR-pathway hypertrophy)
- Higher absolute strength → higher absolute force production capacity → higher peak velocity
- Drag in water is roughly velocity² × frontal area; if velocity gain > drag gain from extra mass, net positive

**IF ALTERNATIVE**:
- Maintain "racing weight" year-round — limits strength ceiling
- Lose weight before race ("cut" strategy) — popular in combat sports, rarely in swim

**EVIDENCE**:
- Force/area calculations are body-shape dependent. McEvoy's 203 cm armspan / 185 cm height = "rectangular" shape may handle weight better (more frontal area normalization) than typical body proportions
- Specific weight-strength-time relationship in swim sprinters: thin literature
- His own n=1: gained 7kg, broke WR. Causal inference impossible without controls.

**CONFIDENCE**: Medium-low. Works for him; uncertain it generalizes. Lighter swimmers may not have the body-shape headroom. **Worth flagging as personality-of-method, not principle**.

## Section 3 — Modality Comparison: Predicted Outcomes

(Synthesis across literature + McEvoy data)

| Dimension | Traditional High-Volume | USRPT (Rushall) | McEvoy Method | Hybrid (mid-vol race-pace + strength) |
|-----------|-------------------------|-----------------|---------------|---------------------------------------|
| Peak velocity | Medium (limited by CNS fatigue) | Medium-high | **High** | High |
| Strength ceiling | Low (concurrent interference) | Low (no strength emphasis) | **Highest** | Medium-high |
| Race fall-off | Low (aerobic base) | Medium | **Medium** (WR data shows similar fall-off) | **Lowest** (predicted) |
| CNS recovery for championships | Worst | Better | **Best** | Better |
| Time to peak adaptation | Slow (3-4 yr) | Medium | **Medium** (validated 18-24 mo) | Unknown |
| Injury risk (shoulder) | Highest (volume-driven) | Medium | Low (low volume) | Low-medium |
| Age compatibility | Bad after 28 | OK | **Best for 30+** | Good |
| Junior compatibility | Best (technique development) | OK | **Risky** (no foundation) | Best balance |
| Generalizability across event | Good (any distance) | Sprint-only | **Sprint-only** | Sprint + 100m |

**The interesting prediction**: A hybrid (10-15 km/wk race-pace-only + McEvoy strength + French Contrast) might match or beat pure McEvoy on second-half fall-off while preserving the strength advantage. Nobody has tested this at elite level.

## Section 4 — ที่ "ถูกต้องที่สุด" — Convergent vs Contested Claims

### CONVERGENT (3+ independent sources, peer-reviewed support, high confidence)

1. **Upper-body Pmax (bench press load-velocity profile) is the strongest dryland predictor of 50m freestyle time**. r=-0.86 in 36 swimmers (Aouani 2025); converges with Hawley 1992 (r=0.82-0.93), Crowley 2017 systematic review (r=-0.68 to -0.79). [HIGH]
2. **French Contrast Method improves explosive output more than equal-load or complex training**. 3 RCTs 2025-2026 with η² 0.62-0.85 effect sizes. [HIGH]
3. **Strength-first sequencing optimizes neuromuscular outcomes** for explosive power events. Feng 2026 systematic review of 42 studies. [HIGH]
4. **Volume reduction is viable for elite established 50m specialists** (McEvoy, Moclair, Tim Lane's group). Multiple-source corroboration; magnitude varies. [HIGH for direction, MEDIUM for exact volume]
5. **70%+ of front-crawl propulsion comes from upper limbs** (Toussaint & Truijens 2005); justifies upper-body strength emphasis. [HIGH]
6. **50m freestyle is ATP-PCr + glycolytic dominant; <15% aerobic** (multiple energy-systems references). [HIGH]
7. **Underwater dolphin kick is the fastest segment of a 50m race** when swimmer's underwater speed exceeds surface speed (general consensus + WR data showing 12.3m underwater at 2.98 m/s peak). [HIGH]

### CONTESTED (sources disagree OR strong claim from single source)

1. **"Previous high-volume training base contributes nothing to McEvoy's current success"**. Lane + McEvoy + their advisor say no. SwimSwam commenters cite his elite 100-200 background as foundation. **No experimental data exists or is feasible**. [CONTESTED]
2. **"Aerobic system has zero contribution to 50m freestyle"**. Parigi (Substack) implies near-zero. r/Swimming Pan Zhanle discussions imply some role. Energy systems theory says ~10% — small but non-zero. [CONTESTED — McEvoy's absolutist framing exceeds evidence]
3. **"This method works for non-elite or non-sprint swimmers"**. McEvoy's own caveat: his foundation is 10+ years. Junior corroboration (Moclair, Akira) limited to one coaching group. **Selection bias likely**. [CONTESTED]
4. **"FCM directly extends to swim 50m the way it does to badminton/soccer"**. RCTs are on land sports. Swim-specific FCM RCTs: thin. **Direction probably right; magnitude uncertain**. [HYPOTHESIS-LEVEL]
5. **"1080 Sprint training transfers to race performance better than tow-rope or parachute"**. Equipment manufacturer claim; no comparison RCT. [VENDOR-WEIGHTED, untested]

### HYPOTHESIS-LEVEL (no peer-reviewed support, intuition or n=1 only)

1. **2x bodyweight pull-up is the right strength target for sprint swimmers**. Based on Poliquin lineage tradition; no evidence-based threshold.
2. **Stroke rate train at exactly 58 spm during strength block** (Parigi). Specific number unsupported.
3. **6-month strength block with "almost no swimming" works for any sprinter**. McEvoy's specific n=1; transferability unclear.
4. **Speed-reserve training (95-100% sprints) prevents nervous-system threat-response at 35m mark**. Mechanistic story; no neurophysiological data on swim sprinters specifically.
5. **Climbing/bouldering specifically (vs general grip/pulling work) is what built his pulling base**. Claim from cross-training docs; correlation, not causation.

## Section 6 — Open Questions (Aree can't answer; literature can't either)

1. **Does the second-half fade in McEvoy's WR race indicate aerobic-work would help?** Unresolved.
2. **What's the minimum volume below which technique regresses?** Lane group says ~1 km/wk; no cross-validation.
3. **Can a non-sprinter (e.g., Toey switching from distance to sprint at 28+) replicate his trajectory?** McEvoy himself was 100-200 specialist before; some yardage base exists. Pure-sprint-from-zero unstudied.
4. **How long can the method sustain results?** WR at age 31; pre-Tokyo decline was age-related per traditional method. Multi-year sustainability of low-volume method: 4-6 years observed; long-term not yet known.
5. **Why bench press worked when added at age 31, despite avoiding it career-long?** Could be: (a) just gym hypertrophy adding to his force ceiling; (b) opening a previously underused horizontal-push motor pattern; (c) coincidence with other timing factors.
6. **Is Matt Smith's mobility work (Poliquin-derived) load-bearing in the result, or coincidental?** No way to isolate.
