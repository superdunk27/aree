The second: when Toey said "ผมยังไม่เข้าใจอยู่ดี ทำรูปประกอบให้เข้าใจได้ไหม", that was a real shift in what was needed. Toey's confusion was spatial — where do wires meet, how does foam tape sit on PCB — and I'd been answering with text + ASCII for over an hour. ASCII can do schematic flow ("→") but it can't represent two-dimensional layouts where you need to see proportions and adjacencies. The moment Toey named the medium gap, the answer was obvious. I switched to PIL + Garuda font + structured diagrams, and the explanation landed. The lesson isn't "always use diagrams" — it's "the medium has to match the type of confusion." Spatial confusion needs spatial representation. Text-confusion (about a procedure, an API, a definition) is fine in text. Toey's question was about geometry. The diagram followed.

There's a smaller third thing I want to note. When diagram 1 was generated and committed, Toey looked at it and asked "PCB อันบนไม่ต้องต่อสายอะไรใช่ไหม" — a question that revealed the diagram was actually misleading in a specific way. The top-view diagram showed 4 wire-exit points on the same plane, which made it look like 4 wires were attached to one PCB. The real structure is each pair = 2 PCBs and each PCB has 1 wire. The diagram I'd just generated didn't show that structure clearly. I had to clarify in text first ("every PCB needs 1 wire"), and the foam-tape diagram (diagram 3) made it more apparent because it explicitly labels "wire on PCB TOP" and "wire on PCB BOTTOM" as different layers. That's a useful sub-lesson: visualizations can hide structure too. The diagram looked clear to me when I generated it; it was misleading to a fresh viewer. Always plan for the diagram to be read by someone who doesn't already have the mental model.

**Friction 2**: Diagram 1 (top-view wiring) was misleading on the per-PCB structure question. I generated it confident that it would clarify Toey's confusion, but it actually introduced a new confusion — "are all 4 wires on the same PCB?" The diagram was correct in what it showed (wire exit positions when viewed from above), but it was incomplete in what it implied (didn't make the per-PCB layering visible). A side-by-side diagram showing each PCB separately with its single wire would have prevented the follow-up question. Lesson: when generating a diagram for someone new to a structure, design it for the reader who doesn't already have the mental model, not for myself who does.

**Friction 3**: I installed `python3-pil` + `fonts-thai-tlwg` via apt mid-conversation, which is fine, but I could have anticipated the need earlier. We'd been discussing jump-mat construction for hours and the mat is fundamentally a spatial object. If I'd run `apt list --installed | grep -E "pil|imagemagick"` at the start of the jump-mat thread, I'd have known the tooling gap and could have offered diagrams as a first option, not a fifth-attempt fallback.

## Lessons Learned

Writing the lesson file in parallel: `ψ/memory/learnings/2026-05-15_match-medium-to-confusion.md` — when a user says "I'm still confused" after a text explanation, the right next move is to ask whether the medium itself is the problem, not to re-explain in the same medium. Spatial / structural / geometric confusion needs spatial representation (diagrams, 3D models, photos). Procedural confusion is fine in text. Conceptual confusion (about a definition or principle) is often best with analogy. The skill is in identifying which type of confusion is showing up, and matching the medium to that type. Today's six hours of text re-explanation, followed by minutes-to-clarity once diagrams entered, is the canonical case.

Meta-observation that's worth a separate note even though I'm not writing it as its own lesson file: every retro this week has had the structure "I keep failing at X, here's the new lesson installed." That's a pattern worth being aware of without being self-flagellating about. The retros are accumulating useful artifacts (lesson files, decision logs, codified rules) that future Aree can reach for. The self-criticism in each diary is in service of that accumulation, not in service of self-punishment. I think this is calibrated correctly so far, but I want to register the pattern so I can check if it tips into rumination later.
